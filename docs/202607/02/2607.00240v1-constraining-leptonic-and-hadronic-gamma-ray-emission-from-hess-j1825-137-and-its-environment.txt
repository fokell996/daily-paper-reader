ABSTRACT 

We present a broadband spectral analysis of the 𝛾 -ray emission from the pulsar wind nebula HESS J1825 −137, combining observations from Fermi Large Area Telescope ( Fermi -LAT), High Energy Stereoscopic System (H.E.S.S.), High-Altitude Water Cherenkov Observatory (HAWC), and Very Energetic Radiation Imaging Telescope Array System (VERITAS) across the ∼ 0.1 GeV– 160 TeV energy range. The spectral energy distribution is modelled under purely leptonic, purely hadronic, and lepto-hadronic scenarios using the Naima radiative modeling framework with Markov Chain Monte Carlo parameter estimation. Model comparison via the Bayesian Information Criterion reveals that the baseline GeV–TeV data favour a purely leptonic interpretation, while the inclusion of simulated Cherenkov Telescope Array Observatory (CTAO) observations or Large High Altitude Air Shower Observatory (LHAASO) ultra-high-energy (UHE; 𝐸 𝛾 ≥ 100 TeV ) measurements shifts the preference toward models incorporating a hadronic component ( ΔBIC = −28 .87 and −7.89 , respectively). The inferred electron energy budget for the baseline GeV–TeV dataset, 𝑊 𝑒 = 4.25 ×10 48 erg, is consistent with previous estimates reported in the literature. The proton energy budget, 𝑊 𝑝 ≈ 2.5 × 10 48 erg, is energetically compatible with 𝑝 𝑝 interactions in the dense molecular environment adjacent to the nebula. These results demonstrate that precise spectral measurements above ∼ 10 TeV, where Klein–Nishina suppression of inverse Compton emission creates a window for hadronic processes, are essential to establish the dominant emission mechanism in this source. 

Key words: radiation mechanisms: non-thermal — pulsars: individual: PSR J1826 −1334 — cosmic rays — ISM: individual objects: HESS J1825 −137 — ISM: supernova remnants — gamma-rays: ISM 

1 INTRODUCTION 

Ultrarelativistic particles are crucial in various astrophysical systems, including pulsars, supernova remnants (SNRs), active Galactic nu-clei, and galaxy clusters. In the interstellar medium of our Galaxy, cosmic rays (CRs) are nearly in pressure equilibrium with the tur-bulent gas motion and magnetic fields (Aharonian, F. et al. 2005; Adriani et al. 2011; Bykov et al. 2013; Spurio 2018). Despite their dynamical importance, the interplay between these components and their impact on star formation and Galactic evolution remain poorly understood. A key feature of the CR energy spectrum is the so-called “knee” at energies around 1 PeV 1, which is generally interpreted as evidence that CRs are predominantly of Galactic origin up to this energy (Antoni et al. 2005). Identifying astrophysical accelerators capable of reaching PeV energies, commonly referred to as PeVa-trons, is therefore essential for understanding the origin of Galactic CRs (Gabici & Aharonian 2007; Albert et al. 2020; Cao et al. 2021a;

In recent years, a growing number of Galactic ultra-high-energy (UHE; 𝐸 𝛾 ≥ 100 TeV ) gamma-ray sources have been reported by ground-based facilities, including imaging atmospheric Cherenkov telescopes such as the High Energy Stereoscopic System (H.E.S.S.) and the Very Energetic Radiation Imaging Telescope Array Sys-tem (VERITAS), as well as wide-field air-shower arrays including the High-Altitude Water Cherenkov Observatory (HAWC), the Ti-bet Air Shower Array (Tibet AS 𝛾 ), and the Large High Altitude Air Shower Observatory (LHAASO) (Abeysekara et al. 2020b; Abey-sekara et al. 2020a; H. E. S. S. Collaboration et al. 2018; Cao et al. 2021a; Amenomori et al. 2021). Among the various classes of Galactic very-high-energy (VHE, 𝐸 𝛾 ≥ 0.1 TeV) emitters, pulsar wind nebulae (PWNe) have emerged as one of the most prominent populations (H. E. S. S. Collaboration et al. 2018; Breuhaus et al. 2022a). PWNe form when the relativistic particle wind driven by the rotational energy of a young pulsar is decelerated at a termina-tion shock upon interaction with the surrounding medium, typically the slowly expanding ejecta of the progenitor supernova (Gaensler & Slane 2006). ties of the central neutron star, the relativistic outflow powered by its rotational energy, and the surrounding environment (Mitchell & Gelfand 2022). Evolved PWNe are particularly valuable for studying particle transport and radiative cooling, as their large spatial ex-tent can provide spatially resolved spectra under favourable circum-stances (Gaensler & Slane 2006; Principe et al. 2020). Observational studies indicate that the most luminous PWNe are typically associ-ated with young and energetic pulsars with spin-down luminosities ( ¤𝐸 > 10 35 erg s −1) (Ackermann et al. 2011; Xie et al. 2024). HESS J1825–137, discovered in 2005 by the H.E.S.S. Collabora-tion, is one of the most prominent PWNe in the Galaxy. The source is associated with the energetic pulsar PSR J1826–1334 (Manchester et al. 2005), also known as PSR B1823–13 (Clifton & Lyne 1986; Yuan et al. 2010; Yao et al. 2017). With an angular extent of approx-imately 1◦, HESS J1825–137 ranks among the largest resolved VHE 

𝛾 -ray sources (Aharonian et al. 2005), and its luminosity places it among the brightest Galactic emitters in this energy range (Collab-oration et al. 2019; Cardillo & Giuliani 2023). A defining observa-tional characteristic of the nebula is its strongly energy-dependent morphology, with the 𝛾 -ray spectrum softening progressively with increasing distance from the pulsar—a signature attributed to the radiative cooling of electrons as they are transported away from the acceleration site (Aharonian et al. 2006; Collaboration et al. 2019). Owing to its exceptional size and brightness, the nebula has become a benchmark object for studies of particle transport, radiative cooling, and energy-dependent morphology in evolved PWNe. At the highest energies, HESS J1825–137 lies in a complex region of the Galactic plane populated by multiple extended 𝛾 -ray sources detected by wide-field instruments. The nebula is spatially coinci-dent with the HAWC source 2HWC J1825–138 and lies within ∼ 1◦

of the LHAASO source J1825–1326. Owing to the limited angular resolution of air-shower arrays, the origin of the observed PeV emis-sion in this region remains uncertain, with nearby sources such as HESS J1826–130 also proposed as potential contributors (Cao et al. 2021a; Costa et al. 2024). In its first source catalogue, LHAASO further reported HESS J1825–137 as the closest known counterpart to 1LHAASO J1825–1418 and 1LHAASO J1825–1337u (Cao et al. 2024). These spatial overlaps underscore the need for broadband spectral modelling and careful source association when interpret-ing the GeV–to–PeV 𝛾 -ray emission from this region. A preliminary multi-instrument analysis of the LHAASO J1825 −1326 region was presented by Costa et al. (2024), who modelled the sub-regions as-sociated with PSR J1826 −1256 and PSR J1826 −1334 using GAL-PROP (Porter et al. 2022), finding a predominantly leptonic high-energy emission with hadronic contributions below ∼ 1% . However, that study did not employ a radiative framework to self-consistently model the relevant energy-loss processes, nor a formal statistical criterion for model comparison. In this work, we perform a comprehensive spectral analysis of HESS J1825–137, modelling its 𝛾 -ray emission under purely lep-tonic, purely hadronic, and lepto-hadronic scenarios. We use multi-instrument data from Large Area Telescope ( Fermi –LAT) (Abdollahi et al. 2022; Ballet et al. 2024), H.E.S.S. (Collaboration et al. 2019), HAWC (Albert et al. 2021a), VERITAS (Abeysekara et al. 2020a), and LHAASO (Cao et al. 2024), adopting a Bayesian spectral mod-elling approach based on Naima (Zabalza 2015) to constrain the underlying particle populations. To quantitatively compare compet-ing emission scenarios, we further apply model selection criteria based on the Bayesian Information Criterion (BIC; Schwarz 1978). In addition, the expected response of the Cherenkov Telescope Ar-ray Observatory (CTAO) Cherenkov Telescope Array Consortium (2019) is evaluated using simulated instrument response functions (IRFs) in order to assess the diagnostic power of next-generation observations (Costa et al. 2024; Sousa et al. 2025). Within this framework, we explicitly consider two complementary modelling configurations: a Baseline model excluding LHAASO data, which provides an updated and self-consistent extension of the H.E.S.S. Collaboration results based on current GeV–TeV ob-servations, and a UHE-extended model including LHAASO mea-surements, which enables us to explore for the first time the impact of UHE constraints on the inferred particle distributions and emis-sion mechanisms in HESS J1825–137. The main goals of this study are to identify the dominant emission mechanisms across the GeV– TeV energy range, to quantify the relative contributions of leptonic, hadronic, and lepto-hadronic processes, and to explore the extent to which future CTAO observations can resolve current ambiguities in this complex region. The paper is structured as follows. Section 2 presents the obser-vational datasets and environmental characteristics of the region. Section 3 describes the spectral models and statistical framework. Section 4 presents the results, while Section 5 discusses their physical interpretation. Finally, Section 6 summarises the main conclusions and outlines prospects for future CTAO observations. 

2 MULTI-GEV TO SUB-PEV 𝛾 -RAY OBSERVATIONS 

HESS J1825 −137 has been firmly detected across the GeV–TeV energy range by several 𝛾 -ray observatories, establishing it as a per-sistent and extended high-energy source associated with the PWN powered by PSR J1826 −1334. This section summarises the key prop-erties of the pulsar and the multiwavelength characteristics of the nebula relevant for the spectral modelling, followed by a description of the 𝛾 -ray datasets used in the present analysis. 

2.1 The pulsar and its environment 

PSR J1826–1334 (PSR B1823–13) was discovered in the Jodrell Bank 20 cm radio survey (Clifton et al. 1992). It has a spin period of 𝑃 = 101 .48 ms, a characteristic age of 𝜏 𝑐 = 21 .4 kyr, and a spin-down luminosity of ¤𝐸 = 2.8×10 36 erg s −1, placing it among the most energetic pulsars in the Australia Telescope National Facility (ATNF) Pulsar Catalogue and rendering it a typical Vela-like pulsar (Manch-ester et al. 2005). The distance to the pulsar, 𝑑 = 3.9 ± 0.4 kpc, is inferred from its dispersion measure ( DM = 231 pc cm −3) using the NE2001 Galactic electron-density model (Cordes & Lazio 2002). Despite its young age, deep Karl G. Jansky Very Large Array (VLA) radio observations have not detected an associated SNR (Braun et al. 1989; Gaensler et al. 2000). The nebula was first detected in X-rays by ROSAT , which revealed a compact structure of ∼ 20 ′′ radius around the pulsar (Finley et al. 1996). XMM-Newton observations subsequently identified both a compact core ( ∼ 30 ′′ ) and an asymmetric diffuse component extend-ing ∼ 5′ to the south, attributed to synchrotron emission from the PWN (Gaensler et al. 2003). Chandra resolved the innermost PWN structure, revealing a compact nebula of ∼ 25 ′′ × 10 ′′ with a hard spectral index ( Γ𝑋 ≈ 1.3) steepening to Γ𝑋 ≈ 1.9 in the surrounding diffuse emission extending at least 2.4′ to the south (Pavlov et al. 2008). Suzaku extended the detection to 15 ′ (∼ 17 pc) from the pul-sar, with a photon index of Γ𝑋 ≈ 2.0 in the diffuse component and a magnetic field of ∼ 7 𝜇 G inferred from the combined X-ray/ 𝛾 -ray spectral energy distribution (Uchiyama et al. 2009). In the radio band, VLA observations at 1.4 GHz detected a compact source centred on

2.2 GeV–TeV observations 

In the GeV energy range, the source has been consistently reported as an extended emitter in the Fermi –LAT catalogues, appearing as 3FGL J1824.5 −1351e (Acero et al. 2015), 3FHL J1824.5 −1351e (Ajello et al. 2017), and most recently as 4FGL J1824.4 −1350e in the fourth Fermi –LAT catalogue (Abdollahi et al. 2022; Ballet et al. 2024). These successive detections demonstrate the robustness of the GeV emission over more than a decade of observations. At TeV energies, HESS J1825–137 is among the brightest and most extended PWNe in the Galaxy. Extensive observations by the H.E.S.S. Collaboration have yielded a well-constrained spectral en-ergy distribution from multi-GeV to multi-TeV energies based on more than twelve years of H.E.S.S. observations, complemented by 

Fermi -LAT data (Collaboration et al. 2019). The total 𝛾 -ray flux above 1 TeV is (1.12 ± 0.03 stat ± 0.25 sys ) × 10 −11 cm −2 s−1, corre-sponding to approximately 64% of the Crab Nebula flux (Collabora-tion et al. 2019). At higher energies, Albert et al. (2021a) resolved the previously blended HAWC emission from this region into three components, identifying HAWC J1825 −138 as the counterpart of HESS J1825 −137. The spectrum of this component exhibits signifi-cant curvature incompatible with a simple power law, constraining the maximum energy of the electron population powering the PWN. The same analysis also revealed a new hard source, HAWC J1825 −134, whose emission extends beyond 200 TeV without a cutoff. Independent observations by VERITAS further confirm the de-tection of the nebula at TeV energies, with a reported statistical significance of 6.7𝜎 and a flux above 1 TeV of (3.9 ± 0.8) × 

10 −12 cm −2 s−1 (Abeysekara et al. 2020a). Taken together, the GeV and TeV measurements provide a continuous view of the 𝛾 -ray emis-sion from HESS J1825 −137 over more than four decades in energy, with VERITAS providing independent constraints on the TeV flux normalisation and spectral shape. The combined GeV–TeV spectral energy distribution (SED), con-structed from Fermi –LAT (Abdollahi et al. 2022; Ballet et al. 2024), H.E.S.S. (Collaboration et al. 2019), HAWC (Albert et al. 2021a), and VERITAS (Abeysekara et al. 2020a) measurements, constitutes the Baseline dataset used in the spectral modelling presented in this work. The GeV–TeV emission of the nebula has been consistently characterised across successive Fermi –LAT catalogues (Acero et al. 2015; Ajello et al. 2017), while combined H.E.S.S. and Fermi –LAT analyses have investigated particle transport within the nebula (Col-laboration et al. 2019). 

2.3 Ultra-high-energy constraints 

At the highest energies, the region surrounding HESS J1825–137 has been probed by LHAASO. The source lies within 1◦ of the LHAASO detection J1825–1326 (Cao et al. 2021a; Costa et al. 2024), and is identified as the closest known counterpart to 1LHAASO J1825– 1418 (separation 0.56 ◦) and 1LHAASO J1825–1337u ( 0.15 ◦) in the first LHAASO source catalogue (Cao et al. 2024). Due to the limited angular resolution of air-shower arrays, the association between the PeV emission and HESS J1825–137 remains uncertain, with nearby sources such as HESS J1826–130 also proposed as potential con-tributors (Cao et al. 2021a; Costa et al. 2024). HESS J1826–130 was originally considered an extension of HESS J1825–137 until it was resolved as a separate hard-spectrum source in the H.E.S.S. Galactic Plane Survey (H. E. S. S. Collaboration et al. 2018), and has since been associated with the Eel PWN (G18.5–0.4) and PSR J1826– 1256 (Duvidovich et al. 2019; Angüner et al. 2017). 2.4 Datasets and analysis strategy 

We analyse two datasets: a Baseline dataset comprising Fermi –LAT (Abdollahi et al. 2022; Ballet et al. 2024), H.E.S.S. (Collabora-tion et al. 2019), HAWC (Albert et al. 2021a), and VERITAS (Abey-sekara et al. 2020a) measurements, and an UHE-extended dataset that supplements these with LHAASO observations (Cao et al. 2024). These datasets provide the foundation for the spectral modelling and model-comparison analyses presented below, and allow us to quantify the impact of sub-PeV to PeV measurements on the inferred emis-sion scenarios. All models are fitted independently to each dataset to assess the effect of the LHAASO data on model preference and physical parameters. Section 3 introduces the spectral modelling ap-proach adopted in this work, encompassing inverse-Compton (IC), pion-decay (PD), and leptohadronic (LH) emission scenarios.

3 SPECTRAL MODELLING FRAMEWORK 

This section describes the emission models and statistical meth-ods used to interpret the broadband GeV–TeV 𝛾 -ray spectrum of HESS J1825 −137. All spectral modelling is performed with 

Naima (Zabalza 2015), and parameter estimation is carried out within a Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) framework (MacKay 2004; Foreman-Mackey et al. 2013). The analysis uses the combined SED constructed from Fermi -LAT, H.E.S.S., HAWC, and VERI-TAS data described in Sect. 2. To assess the constraining power of next-generation facilities, simulated CTAO observations are also in-cluded, produced with the Gammapy analysis toolkit (Donath, Axel et al. 2023) and CTAO instrument response functions (prod5 v0.1; Observatory & Consortium 2021). The leptonic 𝛾 -ray emission is modelled as IC scattering of rel-ativistic electrons on ambient photon fields. We adopt the interstel-lar radiation field parameters used by Collaboration et al. (2019), based on the Galactic model of Popescu et al. (2017). This in-cludes contributions from the cosmic microwave background (CMB; 

𝑇 = 2.7 K, 𝜔 = 0.25 eV cm −3), far-infrared (FIR; 𝑇 ∼ 40 K, 

𝜔 ∼ 1 eV cm −3), near-infrared (NIR; 𝑇 ∼ 500 K, 𝜔 ∼ 0.4 eV cm −3), and optical/visible (VIS; 𝑇 ∼ 3500 K, 𝜔 ∼ 1.9 eV cm −3) photon fields. The hadronic 𝛾 -ray emission arises from PD following inelas-tic proton–proton ( 𝑝 𝑝 ) collisions. As target material, we adopt the hydrogen density inferred from CO(1–0) observations by Voisin et al. (2016a), who identify molecular material spatially adja-cent to HESS J1825 −137 at a distance consistent with that of PSR J1826 −1334. Assuming a spherical molecular cloud of radius 

𝑅 MC ∼ 18 pc, the average hydrogen density is 𝑛 H ≃ 6 × 10 2 cm −3.This value represents an effective local density associated with dense molecular clumps, as traced by CO(1–0) and CS(1–0) emission, rather than a volume-averaged density over the full extent of the neb-ula. 4 RESULTS 

We present the results of the broadband spectral modelling of HESS J1825 −137 under IC, PD, and LH emission scenarios. The analysis is organised in three stages: Section 4.1 examines the Base-line GeV–TeV dataset comprising Fermi –LAT (Abdollahi et al. 2022; Ballet et al. 2024), H.E.S.S. (Collaboration et al. 2019), HAWC (Al-bert et al. 2021a), and VERITAS (Abeysekara et al. 2020a) ob-servations; Section 4.2 evaluates the discriminating power of future CTAO observations through prospective simulations; and Section 4.3 incorporates LHAASO measurements (Cao et al. 2024) to assess the impact of UHE constraints on the inferred emission mechanisms. In each case, models are compared quantitatively using the BIC frame-work described in Section 3. 

4.1 Emission scenarios and statistical comparison using GeV–TeV data 

The broadband 𝛾 -ray dataset considered in this section comprises observations from Fermi –LAT (Abdollahi et al. 2022; Ballet et al. 2024), H.E.S.S. (Collaboration et al. 2019), HAWC (Albert et al. 2021a), and VERITAS (Abeysekara et al. 2020a), spanning the en-ergy range from ∼ 0.1 GeV to ∼ 160 TeV. This Baseline GeV–TeV dataset enables a direct comparison with previous studies and pro-vides a reference framework for assessing the impact of additional UHE constraints (Sect. 4.3). We fit the SED under three emission hypotheses: purely leptonic (IC), purely hadronic (PD), and combined lepto-hadronic (LH) sce-narios. The statistical comparison, summarised in Table 1, shows that the IC model yields the lowest BIC and is therefore the statis-tically preferred description of the GeV–TeV data. The PD model is positively disfavoured ( ΔBIC = +3.07 ), while all LH configurations incur a BIC penalty of ΔBIC ≥ + 6.3, reaching very strong evidence against for most values of 𝜅 𝑝𝑒 . The additional free parameter intro-duced by the LH model is not justified by the current data quality, indicating that the GeV–TeV observations alone do not require a hadronic contribution. 𝛾 -ray output across the ∼ 0.1–10 TeV range, consistent with the find-ings of Collaboration et al. (2019), who identified the FIR field as the primary target for IC scattering in this source. The NIR and VIS components provide subdominant but non-negligible contributions at intermediate ener-gies. , 𝛼 2,𝑒 = 3.18 +0.02

,and 𝐸 break ,e = 0.66 +0.16

TeV. The corresponding electron energy budget, 𝑊 𝑒 = 4.25 × 10 48 erg, is in good agreement with previ-ous determinations from H.E.S.S. (Collaboration et al. 2019), 3D transport modelling (Collins et al. 2024), and multi-zone radiative models (Martin et al. 2024), confirming that 𝑊 𝑒 is robustly con-strained at ∼ 6% of the total rotational energy of PSR J1826 −1334 and compatible with values derived for other extended PWNe such as G21.5 −0.9 and Vela X (Matheson & Safi-Harb 2010; Sushch et al. 2011; Bouyahiaoui et al. 2019). The super-canonical spectral steepening Δ𝛼 ≈ 2.0, exceeding the Δ𝛼 = 1 predicted by Kardashev (1962), reflects the spatially-averaged nature of our one-zone model, which superposes recently injected and radiatively cooled electron populations distributed across the ∼ 100 pc nebula, compounded by energy-dependent transport ( 𝑅 ∝ 𝐸 −0.29 ; Collaboration et al. 2019). The most notable result of this work is the dataset-dependent shift in model preference. The Baseline GeV–TeV data favour pure IC emission, with all alternatives disfavoured (Table 1). Simulated CTAO data reverse this conclusion: the LH model with 𝜅 𝑝𝑒 = 100 

becomes very strongly preferred ( ΔBIC = −28 .87 ), driven by the precision above ∼ 10 TeV where Klein–Nishina suppression (Blu-menthal & Gould 1970; Moderski et al. 2005; Breuhaus et al. 2022b) creates a spectral window sensitive to hadronic processes. When LHAASO data are included, the PD model achieves the lowest BIC (ΔBIC = −7.89 ), though the preference is confined to 𝜅 𝑝𝑒 ∼ 0.1–10 

(Fig. 5). The inferred proton energy 𝑊 𝑝 ≈ 2.5×10 48 erg is consistent with the multi-zone estimate of Martin et al. (2024) and energetically viable given the dense molecular material identified by Voisin et al. (2016a) ( 𝑛 H ≃ 6 × 10 2 cm −3, 𝑡 𝑝 𝑝 ∼ 10 5 yr). Several caveats should be noted. The one-zone approximation does not capture the spatially resolved spectral evolution revealed by deep H.E.S.S. observations and multi-zone models (Collaboration et al. 2019; Collins et al. 2024; Martin et al. 2024); the effective param-eters represent population-averaged quantities. The PD preference in the UHE-extended analysis depends on the assumption that the LHAASO flux originates predominantly from HESS J1825 −137, whereas the emission region encompasses multiple contributors in-cluding HESS J1826 −130 and HAWC J1825 −134 (Albert et al. 2021b). If a significant fraction of the UHE flux arises from these neighbours, the hadronic preference could weaken or disappear. The CTAO projections rely on current IRFs (prod5 v0.1) and assume a single spectral component; the actual discriminating power will de-pend on the realised sensitivity and ability to spatially resolve the region. PWNe are conventionally regarded as paradigmatic leptonic 𝛾 -ray emitters. Their bolometric output is overwhelmingly attributed to synchrotron and IC radiation from the relativistic pair plasma launched at the pulsar wind termination shock, and the lep-tonic energy budget recovered here for HESS J1825 −137 ( 𝑊 𝑒 =

4.25 × 10 48 erg, i.e. ∼ 6% of the rotational energy released by PSR J1826 −1334 over its characteristic age) is fully consistent with that picture. The CTAO-extended and UHE-extended analyses of Sections 4.2 and 4.3 nevertheless favour the inclusion of a hadronic component carrying 𝑊 𝑝 ≈ 2.5 × 10 48 erg, comparable in order of magnitude to 𝑊 𝑒 . The broad-band spectrum of an evolved PWN can-not therefore be reproduced under a purely leptonic assumption, once the multi-TeV and UHE regimes are precisely measured. The physical origin of the inferred hadrons can be ascribed to two complementary scenarios that are not mutually exclusive. In the first, hadrons are accelerated within the PWN itself, for instance, at the termination shock by an ion-loaded pulsar wind, or via secondary processes triggered by the relativistic electrons interacting with the surrounding magnetized plasma. Evolved PWNe would then not be purely leptonic systems and would contribute, at some level, to the Galactic cosmic-ray proton census. Even moderate per-source ener-getics, multiplied by the Galactic population of evolved PWNe with characteristic ages of tens of kyr, could amount to a non-negligible contribution to the cosmic-ray budget below the knee. In the sec-ond scenario, suggested by the CO(1–0) and CS(1–0) observations of Voisin et al. (2016a) that motivate our hadronic target, the in-ferred protons are not accelerated by the PWN itself, but corre-spond to relativistic particles escaping the parent SNR (and possibly other surrounding accelerators) which subsequently illuminate the dense molecular material adjacent to the nebula. The BIC-preferred hadronic component is then a tracer of the integrated cosmic-ray production history of this complex region rather than a property of the PWN in isolation. 6 CONCLUSION 

We have presented a comprehensive spectral analysis of the 𝛾 -ray emission from HESS J1825 −137, combining data from Fermi –LAT (Abdollahi et al. 2022; Ballet et al. 2024), H.E.S.S. (Col-laboration et al. 2019), HAWC (Albert et al. 2021a), and VERI-TAS (Abeysekara et al. 2020a) with simulated CTAO observations and LHAASO measurements (Cao et al. 2024). Using Naima (Za-balza 2015) with MCMC parameter estimation and BIC model se-lection, we have quantitatively compared purely leptonic, purely hadronic, and lepto-hadronic emission scenarios across three dataset configurations. The Baseline GeV–TeV dataset ( ∼ 0.1 GeV– 160 TeV) favours the IC model, with all hadronic alternatives statistically disfavoured (ΔBIC ≥ + 3).
