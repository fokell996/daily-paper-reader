ABSTRACT We present the results of a search for continuous gravitational waves from the low-mass X-ray binary Scorpius X-1 using LIGO data from the first part of the fourth LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA observing run. By applying the resampling version of the cross-correlation pipeline to search for signal frequencies f0

between 25 and 200 Hz (corresponding to neutron star spin frequencies of 12 .5 to 100 Hz for GW due to triaxiality, or ∼ 15 − 20 to ∼ 120 − 150 Hz for GW due to r-modes), we set upper limits below the standard torque balance level, independent of neutron star spin inclination, for 50 Hz ≲ f0 ≲ 200 Hz. While uncertainties in the modelling of torque and equation of state limit the strength of our inference, our results nonetheless argue against torque balance in this spin range for a neutron star described by a hadronic equation of state. The most sensitive upper limits on the gravitational wave amplitude h0, at the upper end of the frequency band searched, approach 5 × 10 −26 marginalized over inclination angle and 2 × 10 −26 assuming the most favorable inclination. The marginalized upper limits correspond to a sensitivity depth of 70 − 75 Hz −1/2, improving sensitivity considerably over previous searches. Expressed as constraints on the triaxial deformation of the neutron star, the limits correspond to an ellipticity of 3 × 10 −5 if the GW frequency f0 is 75 Hz and 3 × 10 −6 if f0 = 200 Hz, approaching deformations which could be supported by ordinary nuclear matter. Outliers from the search were ruled out as potential signals by a combination of hierarchical followup and analysis of additional data from later in the observing run.

INTRODUCTION Neutron stars (NSs) in low-mass X-ray binaries (LMXBs) are prime targets for continuous gravitational wave (GW) searches with the network of current and near-future ground-based audio band detectors, because of arguments that they will soon be detectable (Riles 2023; Owen 2026; Owen & Rajbhandari 2025). In these systems, asymmetries in the NS’s mass distribution, sus-tained by elastic and magnetic stresses (Ushomirsky et al. 2000; Vigelius & Melatos 2009; Priymak et al. 2011; Singh et al. 2020; Osborne & Jones 2020; Pagliaro et al. 2025), can emit a persistent, quasimonochromatic GW signal as the NS rotates. Magnetically channeled accretion provides an additional mechanism to form such a mountain (Melatos & Payne 2005). Accretion can also spin the NS up to a point where gravitational radiation reaction may drive the growth of NS oscillations, such as 

r-modes (Owen et al. 1998; Lindblom et al. 1998; Levin 1999; Rezzolla et al. 2000; Dong & Melatos 2025), which emit continuous GW signals (Riles 2023). The GW torque may offer the solution to an astro-physical conundrum. There appears to be a sharp ob-served cut-off in the spin frequency ( νs) distribution of NSs in LMXBs at νs ≈ 750 Hz (Chakrabarty et al. 2003; Patruno et al. 2017), significantly below the theoreti-cal breakup frequency (Cook et al. 1994; Haskell et al. 2018). Although there are uncertainties when modelling the spin-up accretion torque (Glampedakis & Suvorov 2021; Patruno & Watts 2021), which may explain this observation (Patruno et al. 2012; Ertan & Alpar 2021), it has also been suggested that the spin-down GW torque leads to an equilibrium at high frequencies, which nat-urally explains the observed νs distribution (Papaloizou & Pringle 1978; Wagoner 1984; Bildsten 1998), notably the clustering for 500 Hz ≲ νs ≲ 750 Hz (Patruno et al. arXiv:2607.07765v1 [astro-ph.HE] 8 Jul 2026 2

In such a scenario, there is a natural correlation between the observed X-ray flux and GW strain, as a higher accretion rate exerts a stronger spin-up torque which implies a higher GW torque at equilibrium. Scorpius X-1 (Sco X-1), the most luminous LMXB, which is presumed to consist of an NS of mass ≈ 1.4M⊙

in a binary orbit with a companion star of mass ≈ 0.4M⊙

(Steeghs & Casares 2002), is therefore a promising po-tential source of GWs. In particular, the torque balance model means that searches for GWs from Sco X-1 are now entering the realm where a detectable signal ampli-tude is not just possible but predicted. Sco X-1 has been the target of numerous GW searches (Abbott et al. 2007a,b; Abadie et al. 2011; Aasi et al. 2014, 2015a; Meadors et al. 2017; Abbott et al. 2017a, 2019a, 2021; Abac et al. 2025a; Amicucci et al. 2025) by various methods (Jaranowski et al. 1998; Ballmer 2006; Messenger & Woan 2007; Goetz & Riles 2011; Sammut et al. 2014; Meadors et al. 2016; Leaci & Prix 2015; Singhal et al. 2019; Mukherjee et al. 2023). The cross-correlation method (Dhurandhar et al. 2008; Whelan et al. 2015) used in the present work is an extension of the radiometer search (Ballmer 2006) to use the sig-nal model of GWs from an LMXB such as Sco X-1 to correlate Fourier data at different times. It has previ-ously been applied to Advanced LIGO data from the first, second and third LIGO-Virgo-KAGRA (LVK) ob-serving runs to set the strongest limits so far on GWs from Sco X-1 (Abbott et al. 2017b; Zhang et al. 2021; Abbott et al. 2022a; Whelan et al. 2023). A comple-mentary method of note is the so-called Viterbi search (Suvorova et al. 2016; Suvorova et al. 2017; Melatos et al. 2021), which uses a hidden Markov model to track spin wandering and has also been applied to Advanced LIGO data from the first three LVK observing runs (Abbott et al. 2017c, 2019b, 2022b). The fourth and most recent observing run of Ad-vanced LIGO (Aasi et al. 2015b; Soni et al. 2025; Capote et al. 2025), Advanced Virgo (Acernese et al. 2015; Acer-nese et al. 2023) and KAGRA (Akutsu et al. 2021), O4 for short, began on 2023–May–24 15:00:00 UTC (GPS 1368975618) and ended on 2025–Nov–18 16:00:00 UTC (GPS 1447516818). The portion before the com-missioning break at 2024–Jan–16 16:00:00 UTC (GPS 1389456018) is named O4a (Abac et al. 2025b). In this paper, we use O4a data from the two LIGO detectors, as Virgo and KAGRA data were offline during O4a. We use the calibrated data (Sun et al. 2020; LVK 2025) which are limited to times when a detector was in scientific observing mode (Soni et al. 2025). Transient instru-mental glitches degrade the sensitivity, so we use the channels H1:GDS-CALIB STRAIN CLEAN GATED G02 and 

L1:GDS-CALIB STRAIN CLEAN GATED G02 which have had the “self-gating” procedure (Davis et al. 2025) ap-plied to remove such glitches. Some of the parameters inferred from electromagnetic observations of Sco X-1 are summarized in Table 1. The orbital eccentricity is assumed to be small (Steeghs & Casares 2002; Wang et al. 2018; Killestein et al. 2023) and is neglected in the present analysis. A non-circular orbit would add two search parameters, namely the eccentricity and the argument of periapse (Messenger 2011; Leaci & Prix 2015; Low & Melatos 2025), the ef-3fects of which are estimated in Appendix A and dis-cussed at the end of Sec. 3. The remainder of the paper is laid out as follows. Sec-tion 2 describes the modelled GW signal from Sco X-1. Section 3 defines the resampling cross-correlation search method used in this paper. Section 4 describes the iden-tification and followup of potential signals. Section 5 sets upper limits on the GW amplitude from Sco X-1. The conclusions are in Sec. 6.

MODEL OF GRAVITATIONAL WAVES FROM SCO X-1 The GW signal from a rotating NS can be mod-elled as “plus” and “cross” polarization components 

h+(t) = A+ cos[Φ( t)] and h×(t) = A× sin[Φ( t)]. The signal received at a detector is (Jaranowski et al. 1998) 

h(t) = F+(t)h+(t) + F×(t)h×(t) , (1) where F+ and F× are antenna response functions for that detector in the preferred polarization basis associ-ated with the source, constructed from amplitude mod-ulation coefficients a(t) and b(t) (which are detector spe-cific and periodic over one sidereal day) and an unknown polarization angle ψ which is specific to the orientation of the NS (Jaranowski et al. 1998; Prix & Whelan 2007). If the NS can be modelled as a non-precessing ro-tating triaxial ellipsoid, so that the GWs are gener-ated by the (non-axisymmetric) equatorial ellipticity of the NS, the GW frequency will be twice the spin fre-quency. If the GWs are generated by r-modes, rotating current perturbations in the NS, the GW frequency is 

∼ 1.39 − 1.64 × the spin frequency (Owen & Rajbhan-dari 2025). Since the signal phase [equation (2)] Φ( t) is periodic in the source frame time t(t), resam-pling (i.e., reconstructing a time series which is evenly sampled in the source frame time t) turns the time in-tegral within each SFT and the sum over SFTs into a Fourier transform, which also replaces the loop over sig-nal frequency f0. Since the SFT durations are limited by Doppler acceleration, the calculation is made more efficient by changing the time baseline for this computa-tion from Tsft to Tmax (after resampling and accounting for the time dependence of the antenna patterns). This means that the data from each instrument are divided into intervals of duration Tmax , and all data lying in in-tervals separated by Tmax or less are combined in the calculation of the statistic, as illustrated in the bottom row of Fig. 1. Unlike in the O1 and O3 analyses of Abbott et al. (2017b, 2022a); Whelan et al. (2023), which used a range of Tmax values from 240 s to 25920 s to tune sensitiv-ity against computing cost, the current search uses a fixed value of Tmax = 24 hr = 86400 s, just as the re-sampling search of Zhang et al. (2021) used a Tmax of 19 hr = 68400 s. The SFT duration Tsft is limited by the Doppler acceleration of the orbit of Sco X-1, which would otherwise cause the GW signal to depart from the monochromatic assumption during an SFT. Optimal choices for Tsft as a function of signal frequency f0 were determined in Whelan et al. (2015) and used in Abbott et al. (2017b, 2022a); Whelan et al. (2023). However, in preparation for the present analysis, two errors were discovered in Figure 4 of Whelan et al. (2015). Most significantly, the assumed projected orbital velocity was taken from the a sin i estimates appearing in e.g., Ab-bott et al. (2007a); Watts et al. (2008), which assumed a one-sigma uncertainty of a sin i = 1 .44 ±0.18 lt-s rather than the broader range of a sin i ∈ [1 .44 , 3.25] lt-s (Wang et al. 2018; Killestein et al. 2023) shown in Table 1. A computational error in the opposite direction somewhat mitigated this, but the end result is that slightly shorter SFTs are appropriate. The Tsft values used in this search are shown in Table 2. 3

From an astrophysical point of view, the upper lim-its from the current search allow us to essentially ex-clude the GW-dominated torque balance scenario in the 12 range between roughly 50 and 200 Hz. On the one side the torque balance amplitude in equation (7) already considers the most stringent upper limit by taking the torque arm to correspond to the stellar surface, and on the other it assumes a very compact neutron star, with mass M = 1 .4M⊙ and radius R = 10 km. Microphysi-cally motivated equations of state (EoSs) generally pre-dict less compact stars, and even for softer EoS, such as for example the GR15 EoS (Gulminelli & Raduta 2015) considered by Abbott et al. (2022a), one cannot construct a model that would allow for torque balance. Although it is important to keep in mind that significant uncertainties are present in torque modelling, and that GW torques may be active only part of the time (Haskell et al. 2015), our results essentially exclude the possibil-ity that Sco X-1 includes an NS in equilibrium between accretion torques and GW torques with a GW frequency between approximately 50 and 200 Hz. (This corre-sponds to a spin frequency νs between 25 and 100 Hz if triaxiality is the dominant GW emission mechanism, or ∼ 15 − 20 and ∼ 120 − 150 Hz if the GWs are driven by r-modes.) The torque balance scenario remains hy-pothetically possible for a very compact quark star, an object which has been conjectured to exist in LMXBs (Gondek-Rosi´ nska et al. 2003). In this case, if for ex-ample we consider a quark star described by the MIT bag model, with a bag constant B = 60 MeV/fm 3, then stars with masses M ≲ 0.5 M⊙ (and corresponding radii 

R ≲ 8 km) would still be consistent with the torque bal-ance scenario and the reported upper limits.

CONCLUSIONS AND OUTLOOK We have presented the results of the most sensitive search to date for GWs from Sco X-1, using LIGO detec-tor data from the first eight months of the fourth observ-ing run of Advanced LIGO, Advanced Virgo, and KA-GRA. Using the resampling CrossCorr pipeline to search with a coherence time of Tmax = 24 hr, we have set upper limits for GW signal frequencies 25 Hz < f 0 < 200 Hz, corresponding to NS spin frequencies of 12 .5 Hz < ν s <

100 Hz (or 15 − 20 Hz ≲ νs ≲ 120 − 150 Hz for r-modes). The sensitivity of the search now surpasses, for the first time the na¨ ıve torque balance prediction for plausible signal strengths regardless of assumptions about the NS inclination angle, for 50 Hz < f 0 < 200 Hz. It is of course quite possible that the spin frequency of Sco X-1 is above 100 Hz, with a GW frequency above 200 Hz. In particular, the distribution of spin frequen-cies of the observed accreting millisecond X-ray pul-sars (AMXPs) appears to be bimodal (Patruno et al. 2017), with a “fast” population of pulsars, narrowly dis-tributed around νs ≈ 550 Hz, which can be explained theoretically by the presence of GW torques (Gittins & Andersson 2019), and would lead to gravitational emis-sion at frequencies as high as 1100 Hz. Forthcoming analysis of additional data from the fourth observing run will apply a variety of search methods which balance sensitivity and robustness to signal model assumptions across a wider range of signal frequencies.
