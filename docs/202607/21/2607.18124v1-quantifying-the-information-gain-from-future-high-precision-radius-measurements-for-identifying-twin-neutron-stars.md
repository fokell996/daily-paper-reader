## 摘要
双中子星（NS）具有相同的引力质量但不同的半径，是超稠密物质中强一阶强子-夸克相变最有希望的天体物理特征之一。我们利用典型$1.4\,M_\odot$中子星的模拟半径数据，研究了越来越精确的中子星半径测量如何改进双星可观测性的贝叶斯推断。半径不确定性从当前约$0.9$ km的水平变化到未来X射线和引力波观测所预期的约$0.1$ km精度。我们使用最大双星半径分离$ΔR$的后验分布、分支可区分性的解析模型以及基于分支观测效率和香农熵的互补信息论度量来量化所获得的信息。综合分析揭示了三种推断区域：先验主导区域（$σ_R \gtrsim 0.6$ km）、快速信息增益区域（$0.2 \lesssim σ_R \lesssim 0.6$ km）和信息饱和区域（$σ_R \lesssim 0.2$ km）。这些互补分析一致表明，精度约$0.2$ km的半径测量已经提取了当前贝叶斯框架内识别双中子星所需的大部分信息。除了为未来高精度半径测量建立定量观测基准外，这项工作还提供了一个通用贝叶斯框架，用于量化逐步更精确观测的信息增益，并确定科学回报递减的临界点。

## Abstract
Twin neutron stars (NSs), characterized by identical gravitational masses but different radii, are among the most promising astrophysical signatures of a strong first-order hadron--quark phase transition in supradense matter. We investigate how increasingly precise NS radius measurements improve the Bayesian inference of twin-star observability using mock radius data for a canonical $1.4\,M_\odot$ NS. Radius uncertainties are varied from the current level of about $0.9$ km to the $\approx 0.1$ km precision anticipated from future X-ray and gravitational-wave observations. We quantify the information gained using the posterior distribution of the maximum twin-star radius separation $ΔR$ together with an analytical model of branch distinguishability and complementary information-theoretic measures based on the branch observational efficiency and the Shannon entropy. The combined analyses reveal three inference regimes: a prior-dominated regime for $σ_R \gtrsim 0.6$ km, a rapid information-gain regime for $0.2 \lesssim σ_R \lesssim 0.6$ km, and an information-saturation regime for $σ_R \lesssim 0.2$ km. These complementary analyses consistently indicate that radius measurements with a precision of about $0.2$ km already extract most of the information available for identifying twin NSs within the present Bayesian framework. Beyond establishing a quantitative observational benchmark for future high-precision radius measurements, this work provides a general Bayesian framework for quantifying the information gain from progressively more precise observations and identifying the point of diminishing scientific returns.
