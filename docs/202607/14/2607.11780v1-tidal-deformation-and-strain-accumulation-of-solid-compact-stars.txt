ABSTRACT 

The tidal deformability of compact stars encodes the equation of state of dense matter, and gravitational-wave observations such as GW170817 have begun to constrain it under the assumption of a fluid interior. Yet whether the interior of pulsar-like compact stars is fluid or solid remains largely untested, despite the distinct tidal responses the two states predict. In this work, based on the strangeon-star model, we develop a framework for modeling tidal deformation in solid compact stars. Adopting a shear modulus of 𝜇 = 10 34 erg cm −3, we find a relative difference of approximately 40% in tidal deformability between solid and fluid strangeon stars of 1.4 𝑀 ⊙ , corresponding to a ∼ 10% deviation from the universal I–Love relation. We further model the accumulation of internal strain during binary inspiral and find that it peaks near the stellar center. When the gravitational-wave frequency reaches several hundred Hz , large-scale fracturing occurs and can release up to ∼ 10 46 erg of elastic energy, sufficient to power short 𝛾 -ray-burst precursors. This solid-to-fluid transition alters the tidal response and imprints on the waveform and phase of the emitted gravitational radiation. Combined with the precursor electromagnetic emission, these gravitational-wave signatures offer a multi-messenger avenue to test the solid nature of pulsar-like compact stars. 

Key words: dense matter – equation of state – gravitational waves – stars: interiors – gamma-ray burst: general 

1 INTRODUCTION 

The equation of state (EOS) of matter at super-nuclear densities re-mains an open question. Because quantum chromodynamics is non-perturbative in this regime, the EOS cannot be computed from first principles (Kojo 2019), and many competing models have been pro-posed for the internal composition of pulsar-like compact stars. One influential possibility originates with Witten (1984), who conjec-tured that strange quark matter—composed of nearly equal numbers of 𝑢 , 𝑑 , and 𝑠 quarks—could be more stable than ordinary nuclear matter such as 56 Fe, and might therefore be the true ground state of strongly-interacting matter. Compact stars made of such matter are known as strange quark stars (SqSs). At the densities expected inside an SqS, quark matter may enter a crystalline color-superconducting (CCS) phase (Alford et al. 2001), a supersolid whose shear modulus is roughly 20–1000 times that of a neutron-star crust (Mannarelli et al. 2007). This CCS picture, however, is not the only one. Xu (2009) argued that the density inside pulsar-like compact stars may be only a few times nuclear density, where the color coupling remains strong and the CCS phase is not guaranteed. In this regime quarks tend to condense in position space into clusters that preserve three-light-flavor symmetry (Lai et al. 2022; XU 2025), termed strangeons, and a star built from them is a strangeon star (SnS). Although an SnS

shares its quark-level composition with strange quark matter, the clustered state can have different macroscopic properties (Lai & Xu 2017): in particular, an SnS is expected to be a classical solid (Xu 2004; Lai & Xu 2009), in contrast to the fluid interior of a neutron star and the supersolid interior of an SqS (Lau et al. 2019). Despite their differences, the SnS and SqS hypotheses share a de-cisive feature: a pure, bare solid star—classical solid for the SnS (Xu 2004) and supersolid for the SqS (Lau et al. 2017)—with a large shear modulus of ∼ 10 34 erg cm −3 (Xu 2003; Mannarelli et al. 2007). This is exactly what sets such stars apart from fluid neutron stars, which at most carry a thin solid crust, and it leaves two observable imprints. First, a rigid interior stiffens the star’s tidal response, so a precise measurement of the tidal deformability Λ could tell a solid star from a fluid one. Second, such a star can store substantial elastic energy and release it in starquakes—a mechanism invoked to explain giant flares (Franco et al. 2000; Xu 2003) and the precursors of short 𝛾 -ray bursts (GRBs) (Zhou et al. 2024). In this work we quantify both sig-natures for solid strangeon stars: how the solid nature reshapes the tidal response, and how much elastic energy can be released during a binary inspiral. We take the two signatures in turn. Tidal deformation encodes the internal structure and EOS of a star, and has become directly observable with the rise of gravitational-wave (GW) astronomy: the GW signal from a binary neutron-star merger differs subtly from the binary-black-hole case because matter is present (Flanagan & Hinderer 2008), and this difference is quantified by the tidal de-formability (Damour & Nagar 2009; Postnikov et al. 2010; Hinderer et al. 2010). The detection of GW170817 (Abbott et al. 2017), to-

Whether such a measurement can distinguish a solid star from a fluid one depends on how strongly the shear modulus alters Λ. The relativistic theory of tidal deformation, first developed by Hinderer (2008) and Damour & Nagar (2009), shows that for a neutron star with only a thin solid crust the correction to Λ is negligible ( ∼ 10 −7) (Git-tins et al. 2020); extensive calculations across many EOSs confirm that the deviation from the universal I–Love relation is unobservable for GW detection (Lin 2013; Biswas et al. 2019; Penner et al. 2011; Alford et al. 2008), and even a fluid strangeon star follows nearly the same relation as an ordinary neutron star (Gao et al. 2021). A pure solid star is qualitatively different. For solid SqSs, Lau et al. (2017) found a ∼ 60% deviation, and we show below (Section 3.1) that solid strangeon stars exhibit a ∼ 40% deviation—large enough to be within reach of next-generation GW detectors (Shterenberg & Zhou 2025). The second signature is the storage and release of elastic energy in starquakes. Ruderman (1969) first studied quakes in the solid crust of a neutron star and linked them to the sudden period changes known as glitches, and later work associated the elastic energy re-leased in such events with anomalous X-ray pulsars and soft 𝛾 -ray repeaters (Franco et al. 2000; Ruderman 1991; Perna & Pons 2011), a mechanism that may also operate in SnSs (Xu 2003; Li et al. 2023). Because an SnS is solid throughout rather than only in a thin crust (Xu 2004), it can store far more elastic energy—up to ∼ 10 46 erg even with ∼ 10 −4 anisotropy in pressure, and thus power more energetic events, such as magnetar giant flares (Chen et al. 2024) and GRB precursors. Building on this, Zhou et al. (2024) proposed that the precursor of a short GRB could originate from a tidally induced gi-ant quake in a solid SnS, a scenario they examined quantitatively for GRB 211211A (Xiao et al. 2024), whose precursor is observation-ally confirmed. Competing explanations for such precursors include resonant shattering (Suvorov & Kokkotas 2020; Suvorov et al. 2022; Kuan et al. 2021a,b) and multiple-subjet models (Nakamura 2000); the distinctive claim here is that, for a pure solid star, the stored elastic energy alone can suffice. How much energy is released depends sensitively on the tidal field at the moment of fracturing, so the crucial question is at what inspiral stage large-scale fracturing sets in. Lai et al. (2019) argued that a solid SnS fractures on a large scale once the tidal field drives the internal strain past a critical threshold 𝜎 ; taking 𝜇 = 4 × 10 32 erg cm −3 and 

𝜎 = 0.01 , they found this to occur at a GW frequency of ∼ 20 Hz .Such large-scale fracturing would drive the star from a solid to a fluid-like state, altering its tidal response and imprinting on the GW waveform and phase (Lai et al. 2019). Both the transition frequency and the size of the phase shift depend on 𝜇 and 𝜎 , so the GW signal alone can constrain these two parameters. The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 introduces the mod-eling and theoretical framework for perturbations inside the star, de-riving the differential equations (Section 2.3) and the corresponding boundary conditions (Section 2.4). This section is rather technical; readers primarily interested in the main results may proceed directly to Section 3. We then present numerical results, including the tidal deformability (Section 3.1) and strain accumulation (Section 3.2). Discussion and conclusions are provided in the final section. Unless otherwise noted, geometric units with 𝐺 = 𝑐 = 1 are used throughout this work, where 𝐺 is the gravitational constant and 𝑐 is the speed of light.

4 CONCLUSIONS 

In this paper, we have investigated the tidal deformability and internal strain accumulation of solid strangeon stars. While previous studies of neutron stars with thin solid crusts found negligible deviations in tidal deformability ( < 10 −7) (Gittins et al. 2020; Penner et al. 2011), and Gao et al. (2021) showed that the I–Love relation for fluid SnSs closely follows that of conventional neutron stars, the present work demonstrates that accounting for the fully solid nature of SnSs leads to qualitatively different results. Our key findings are as follows. First, the tidal deformability of solid SnSs differs by approximately 40% from their fluid counter-parts—a discrepancy large enough to produce observable imprints in GW signals (Creci et al. 2025) and that may be accessible with next-generation detectors. This result is consistent with previous studies of solid SqSs (Lau et al. 2019) and extends the analysis to the strangeon star framework. Second, detailed modeling of internal strain accumu-lation reveals that fracturing initiates at the stellar center—contrary to the surface-fracturing assumption of Zhou et al. (2024)—and that large-scale fracturing occurs within the several-hundred-Hz GW fre-

MNRAS 000 , 000–000 (0000) 8 H. Shen et al. 

quency band. Third, the elastic energy released during fracturing is 

∼ 10 45 –10 46 erg at onset and grows as 𝐸 ∝ 𝜇 𝑓 4GW to ∼ 10 49 erg by merger, spanning the energies of observed GRB precursors (Zhou et al. 2024) and FRBs (Wang et al. 2024). Taken together, these signatures are complementary: the precur-sor intensity and waiting time encode the shear modulus 𝜇 ; the solid–fluid difference in tidal deformability provides an independent diagnostic; and the abrupt waveform change at large-scale fractur-ing constrains both 𝜇 and 𝜎 . A multi-messenger observation com-bining the GW signal with the precursor electromagnetic emission could therefore pin down 𝜇 and 𝜎 —or, if the two tests prove mutu-ally inconsistent, rule out the solid-star hypothesis, and with it the strangeon-star conjecture. Several limitations of this work should be noted. The fracturing process and energy release are modeled using a simplified single-layer configuration. A more realistic treatment would require a two-layer structure with a fluid-like core and a solid envelope. As dis-cussed in Section 3.2, the solid core may fracture extensively during the early inspiral and subsequently behave as an effective fluid. Such a two-layer model has been explored for solid SqSs by Lau et al. (2019), who showed that the screening factor gradually decreases as the solid envelope thins. Assuming that this picture extends to SnSs, our single-layer approximation remains valid during most of the in-spiral, when the fluid core radius is below 0.5𝑅 (Lau et al. 2019). Additionally, the breaking strain 𝜎 remains poorly constrained, span-ning several orders of magnitude (Smoluchowski 1970; Caplan et al. 2018). Future work should incorporate a fully time-evolving two-layer simulation and explore how different fracture criteria affect the observable signatures.
