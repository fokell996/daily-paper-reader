Long-period radio transient PSR J0901 −4046 is not an Isolated White Dwarf Pulsar

ABSTRACT We report the Chandra non-detection of PSR J0901 −4046, a P = 75 .89 seconds long-period radio transient (LPT). For a distance of 467 pc, the upper limit on X-ray luminosity is LX ≤ few ×10 28 erg s −1. For the measured P and ˙P , this upper limit, approximately 50 times lower than the previous Swift observations, is comparable to the spin-down luminosity of a neutron star, but would be approximately four orders of magnitude smaller than the spindown power of a white dwarf. Our results disfavor isolated WDs as the central star in PSR J0901 −4046. We suggest that the isolated LPTs are powered by magnetic dissipation (not rotation), in a way similar to magnetars’ radio emission. 

Keywords: High Energy astrophysics (739) — Stellar astronomy (1583) 

1. INTRODUCTION Long-period radio transients (LPTs) are sources of coherent radio pulses with periods ranging from tens of seconds to tens of minutes (Hurley-Walker et al. 2022, 2023; Caleb et al. 2022, 2024; Wang et al. 2025; Lee et al. 2025; Rea et al. 2026). They consist of two distinct classes: (i) accretion-powered binary systems with a white dwarf companion, (ii) more mysterious isolated sources. Both types are often called pulsars, mostly by analogy with periodic emission from rotationally-powered pulsars, but the physical origins of emission in the two cases are likely dramatically different. In case (i), a class of accretion-powered “White Dwarf Pulsars” has been established over the last decade, AR Sco being the first and most famous system (Marsh et al. 2016; Buckley et al. 2017). AR Sco is a 3.56 hour period white dwarf + M dwarf binary at a distance of 116 pc, where the white dwarf has a spin period of 117 s, ˙ ν = −5 × 10 −17 Hz s −1, inferred spin down luminosity of 2 × 10 33 

erg s −1, and x-ray luminosity of LX ∼ 4 × 10 30 erg s −1 (Gaibor et al. 2020; Takata et al. 2021; Pelisoli

Part of the x-ray luminosity is produced at the surface of the donor star, which is not present for an isolated white dwarf pulsar. However, part of the emission is modulated at the spin period, and therefore comes from the white dwarf’s magnetosphere. Other systems that can be classified as accretion-powered white dwarf pulsars include J191213.72 −441045.1 (Pelisoli et al. 2023), dubbed an AR Sco twin. In this white dwarf + M dwarf binary with a 4.03 hour period, a 5.3 min pulsed emission is detected from radio to x-rays. In a follow-up study, Pelisoli et al. (2024) did not find any radio emitting white dwarfs ( ≳1-3 mJy) outside of interacting binaries in the Very Large Array Sky Survey (Gordon et al. 2021). In addi-tion, Zhang et al. (2025) searched for coherent radio signals from five isolated, rapidly rotating, and magnetized white dwarfs, but did not detect any emission down to μJy levels. Hence, we now know two binary white dwarf pulsars, but no rotation-powered (isolated) “White Dwarf Pulsar”. The origin of the second type of long-period radio transients, likely isolated, is more questionable. The key point is that such slowly-rotating regular neutron stars do not have enough electric potential to break vacuum and generate a flow of relativistic pairs, thought to be needed for the generation of pulsar-like radio emission (Goldreich & Julian 1969; Ruderman & Sutherland 1975a; Arons & Scharlemann 1979), see §2. To compensate for slow rotation, super-magnetar magnetic fields would be required (Beniamini et al. 2023; Sob’yanin 2023; Tong 2023). But the longest period case, the 6.45-hour ASKAPJ1839-0756 (Lee et al. 2025), would likely make it unrealistic even for magnetar-scale fields to achieve vacuum breakdown (spindown has not been measured here). An alternative possibility is that the second type of long-period radio transients are isolated white dwarfs. Magnetospheric emission is possible for isolated WDs, as initially argued by Usov (1988); see §2. Detection of non-thermal emission from white dwarfs was long expected, by analogy from other compact objects - neutron stars. Neutron stars produce non-thermal emission via two very differ-ent channels: (i) rotation-powered, mostly isolated neutron stars - pulsars; (ii) accretion-powered (mostly X-ray pulsars). These are two distinct astrophysical phenomena, though there is also a cross-over class of “transitional pulsars”, which show changes from rotation-powered to accretion powered states (Papitto & de Martino 2022). An exciting possibility is a similar dichotomy of rotation-powered/accretion-powered systems translated to white dwarfs.  

> 2.

THE CASE FOR ISOLATED WHITE DWARF PULSARS The existence of rotation-powered white dwarf pulsars has been predicted more than 30 years ago (Usov 1988). At the core is a possibility of particle acceleration and pair production by the rotation induced electric field, similar to the case of neutron stars (Goldreich & Julian 1969). Early models of pair production in the neutron stars’ magnetospheres were based on the curvature emission processes. It is recognized that two-photon pair production can play an important role (e.g., Philippov & Spitkovsky 2018). In the case of white dwarfs with large radii and small magnetic fields, it is the two-photon pair production that may be dominant over curvature emisison. White dwarf pulsars have been hypothesized to provide explanation for some exotic γ-ray and radio sources (Usov 1993; Zhang & Gil 2005), and to contribute to the e± cosmic ray background (Kashiyama et al. 2011). However, no pulsed nonthermal emission from any isolated white dwarf has been discovered so far (Pelisoli et al. 2024), neither in radio nor in X-rays. 3A key prediction of rotation-powered white dwarf emission is the production of high energy emission. In case of pulsars, the radio, high energy and very high energy detections are highly overlapping. The basic reason is that vacuum break-down is always accompanied by high energy emission. An important astrophysical question is whether an isolated white dwarf can produce nonthermal emission - a true, isolated White Dwarf Pulsar . The known population of white dwarfs includes a significant fraction of high-mass ( M > 1 M⊙) products of binary mergers (Cheng et al. 2020; Temmink et al. 2020; Jewett et al. 2024), which, because of their evolutionary history, tend to be rapid rotators (Schwab 2021), and to possess high magnetic fields (Garc´ ıa-Berro et al. 2012). These stars can, in principle, emit coherent radiation in a way analogous to the (still unknown) pulsar emission mechanism (Usov 1988). Zhang et al. (2025) performed a sensitive radio search for periodic pulses from five massive, magnetic, and rapidly rotating isolated white dwarfs, and ruled out emission down to μJy levels. However, given the relatively small sample size and the typical beaming fractions of ∼ 5 − 20% in radio pulsars (Karastergiou & Johnston 2007), geometric effects alone can account for the non-detection of radio pulses if the emission is narrowly beamed. Based on their null result, Zhang et al. (2025) conclude that for the isolated white dwarf pulsar hypothesis, either the radio emission is narrowly beamed or intrinsically weak in these systems. The recent discovery of an extremely slow pulsar, PSR J0901 −4046, with a 75.9 s period and surface field of 1 .4 × 10 14 G (Caleb et al. 2022), renews interest in the possibility of isolated white dwarfs producing coherent radio emission. The timing analysis rules out binarity in PSR J0901 −4046. The discovery of such a slow pulsar is a truly exceptional, but puzzling result; the requirement of a potential drop, Φ > 10 12 V, needed for the e±-pair production and generation of the coherent radio emission (Ruderman & Sutherland 1975b) is clearly violated for this pulsar, which has Φ ∼ 2×10 11 V. PSR J0901 −4046 is clearly unusual among the radio emitting neutron star population: this indicates a possible existence of a new class of previously unobserved, very slowly rotating pulsars. Theoretically, there is a concept of a death line in pulsars, see Fig. 1 in Caleb et al. (2022). CHANDRA OBSERVATIONS OF PSR J0901 −4046 The (presumed neutron star) radio pulsar J0901–4046 (Caleb et al. 2022) has a spin period of P =75 .89 s, period derivative ˙P = 2 .25 × 10 −13 , and thus an inferred dipole magnetic field B = 2 .6 × 10 14 

G. The corresponding spin-down luminosity is Lsd = 2 .0 × 10 28 erg s −1. Caleb et al. (2022) inferred a distance of 328 to 467 pc from its measured dispersion measure of 52 ±1 pc cm −3, using the two alternative Galactic free electron density models of Yao et al. (2017) or Cordes & Lazio (2002). We obtained two Chandra X-ray Observatory (Weisskopf et al. 2002) observations of PSR J0901 −4046, 24.7 ks on 2025 March 13 and 16.7 ks on 2025 August 25. We placed ACIS-S at the focus, in Very Faint mode. We extracted counts and spectra from 1.5 arcsec radii at the location of the pulsar, but detect no photons within a 1.5 arcsec error circle of the pulsar in 0.5-8 keV. We use this to estimate an upper limit on the X-ray luminosity. Gehrels (1986) gives a 99% confidence upper limit of 4.6 counts (per time interval) as the average, if 0 counts are detected in that interval. Or we can use a 95% confidence upper limit of 3.0 counts for 0 counts detected. For a combined exposure time of 41413 seconds, these give upper limits on count rates of 1 .1 × 10 −4 or 7 .2 × 10 −5 cts s −1. Assuming a Γ = 2 power law, and low ( < 10 21 

cm −2) hydrogen column density N H, these give upper limits in 0.5-10 keV flux of Fx < 2.7 × 10 −15 ,or 1 .8 × 10 −15 , erg cm −2 s−1 (for the 99% and 95% confidence upper limits, respectively). A 200 eV blackbody similarly gives Fx < 4.2 × 10 −15 , or < 2.8 × 10 −15 erg cm −2 s−1. These spectral models span the range of observed millisecond pulsar spectra (Bogdanov et al. 2006b), while the NH estimate of < 10 21 cm −2 is justified by its close proximity to the Sun. For an upper limit distance of 467 pc, these estimates give Lx(0.5-10 keV) < 7.0 × 10 28 (PL, 99% conf), < 4.7 × 10 28 (PL, 95% conf), 1 .1 × 10 29 (BB, 99% conf), or 7 .3 × 10 28 erg s −1 (BB, 95% conf). 5If the distance is underestimated by a factor of 3, then these upper limits increase by a factor of 9. These limits are all well below the projected LX for a white dwarf pulsar, even if the distance is substantially underestimated (unlikely given the proximity of the pulsar, which places it in a well-sampled part of the free electron density models). Overall, our upper limit is ∼ 50 times lower than that of Caleb et al. (2022).  

> 4.

DISCUSSION Our non-detection of X-rays disfavors the isolated WD interpretation of J0901–4046. This is also consistent with the optical non-detection of a WD in this system. Caleb et al. (2022) obtained optical photometry of the field around PSR J0901 −4046 with the SAAO 1-m telescope, and ruled out optical counterparts brighter than 20 - 21 mag. The DECam Plane Survey provided grizY band imaging of the same field, which rules out optical counterparts brighter than 23.5 mag in g at the 5 σ level (Saydjari et al. 2023). However, given the relatively high extinction of E(B − V ) = 0 .73 mag towards PSR J0901 −4046 and the fact that distances derived from the dispersion measure can be off by a factor of 2-3 (Deller et al. 2009), it would be possible to hide white dwarfs more massive than about 1.1 M⊙ under the worst case scenario (a 3 × further distance of ∼ 1.5 kpc). Regardless of these issues, the newly obtained Chandra data favor the neutron star, rather than the white dwarf, interpretation of PSR J0901-4046. A binary WD is also excluded by the phase-connected timing reported by Caleb et al. (2022). Our results further deepen the mystery of long period transients. The key question is the origin of high energy particles that produce radio emission, and the origin of the electric potential that accelerates these particles. Isolated neutron stars self-produce these particles in vacuum breakdown using rotationally-induced EMF, while in binary systems particles are injected from the compan-ion’s wind, while acceleration relies, eventually, on the EMF of the wind ( e.g. Jovian hectometric radio emission, Hess et al. 2014). In the case of isolated neutron star PSR J0901-4046, both these mechanisms are excluded. A remaining possibility is a Solar-flare paradigm, whereby the required accelerating potential is achieved in magnetic reconnection events in magnetar magnetospheres (not rotationally-powered, Lyutikov 2002; Thompson et al. 2002; Lyutikov 2003; Kaspi & Beloborodov 2017). Magnetars’ radio emission clearly shows transient properties (Camilo et al. 2006). Thus, magnetically-powered radio emission has a clear prediction: LPTs should show transient radio emis-sion as the reconnection sites in the magnetosphere are activated.
