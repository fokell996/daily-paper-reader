## Bayesian inference of the dense matter equation of state built upon extended Skyrme interactions: A generalization 

Mikhail V. Beznogov 1, ∗ and Adriana R. Raduta 1, †

> 1

National Institute for Physics and Nuclear Engineering (IFIN-HH), RO-077125 Bucharest, Romania 

(Dated: July 13, 2026) The nonrelativistic theory of nuclear matter (NM) based on Brussels-Skyrme interactions is employed to develop models for dense and neutron-rich matter within a Bayesian framework. We employ the following set of constraints: the four best-known nuclear empirical parameters, density dependence of the energy per particle in pure neutron matter (PNM), density dependence of the Landau e ff ective mass ( meff ) of neutrons in PNM and symmetric NM, and a lower limit on the maximum gravitational mass that neutron stars (NSs) can sustain. In addition, a number of “sanity checks” are added: the values of the speed of sound, neutron and proton Landau e ff ective masses and Fermi velocities are constrained up to the central density of the most massive NS configuration and for isospin asymmetries δ = (nn − np)/(nn + np) ranging from 0 to 1. Our ensemble of models 

fully explores the capacity of non-relativistic Brussels-Skyrme e ff ective interactions to describe NM at densities exceeding several times the nuclear saturation density. This is a necessary step toward a better understanding of the properties of dense matter and possible correlations between the parameters of NSs and the parameters of NM. Due to pronounced U-shaped density-dependencies of meff , all our models exhibit a non-monotonic “rise-and-fall” behavior of the thermal pressure ( pth ) as a function of density, which in extreme cases leads to pth < 0. This work is a generalization of [Beznogov and Raduta, Phys. Rev. C 110, 035805 (2024)]. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

The last decade has seen unprecedented progress in the multi-messenger astronomy of neutron stars (NSs). Precise measurements of massive pulsars’ masses [ 1, 2], estimates of NSs’ radii via x-ray pulse profile modeling [ 3– 10 ], and the inference of (combined) tidal deformability of NSs via gravita-tional wave (GW) detection [ 11 – 13 ] have opened new avenues for understanding matter at extreme densities and isospin asym-metries that are not accessible in terrestrial laboratories. As such, a large variety of phenomenological models, tra-ditionally employed to address properties of finite nuclei and nuclear matter (NM), e.g., non-relativistic mean-field models with zero-range interactions [ 14 – 17 ] and relativistic mean-field models with non-linear and /or density dependent cou-plings [ 15 , 18 – 24 ], were used to study the behavior of dense and neutron-rich NM, compatibility with available astrophysics constraints and possible drawbacks caused by the use of en-ergy density functionals (EDF) of limited flexibility. All these recent studies account for constraints from nuclear physics experiments, most often translated into values that the nuclear empirical parameters (NEPs) take, ab-initio calculations of the energy per particle in pure neutron matter (PNM) with densities up to the saturation density of symmetric matter (nsat ∼ 0.16 fm −3 ∼ 2 · 10 14 g/cm 3), and a lower bound-ary on the maximum gravitational mass of NSs of ∼ 2 M⊙.In some cases, constraints from measurements of NSs’ radii and tidal deformabilities or results of perturbative quantum chromodynamics, valid for n ∼ 40 nsat , were included, too. The heterogeneity of these constraints, combined with their dissimilar and poorly understood sensitivities to various den-sity domains and isospin asymmetries and with the convoluted

The aim of this paper is to extend and generalize the work done in Refs. [ 16 , 17 ] hereafter referred to as Paper I and Pa-per II, respectively. Paper I focused on the Bayesian inference of the equation of state (EoS) of dense matter using EoS mod-els built within the non-relativistic mean-field model of NM with standard Skyrme interactions [ 25 ]. It uses a minimal set of constraints that consists of the four best known NEPs, the density dependence of the energy per neutron ( E/A) in PNM and a lower limit on the maximum mass of NSs in addition to physicality conditions on NS matter, i.e., thermodynami-cal stability and causality. In some runs, we also constrained the values of neutron e ff ective masses ( meff ;n) in symmetric nuclear matter (SNM) and PNM at n = 0.16 fm −3, while in others we accounted for correlations between the values that 

E/A in PNM takes at di ff erent densities. A significant number of EoS models thus generated feature nucleon e ff ective masses that, for densities in excess of a few times nsat , reach values as low as ∼ 0.1mn, where mn stands for the bare mass of the neu-tron, and /or symmetry energies ( Esym ) that have rise-and-fall behavior as a function of density ( n). A significant drawback of these low meff ;n values is that the Fermi velocity vF;n can exceed the speed of light ( c) [ 26 ], thereby rendering the model unphysical. Low values of Esym are incompatible with direct Urca operating in nucleonic stars [ 27 ]. Moreover, rise-and-fall behaviors for Esym (n) are in tension with the predictions of relativistic mean-field models where the same constraints are used [19–21, 23, 24]. Paper II repeated the analysis of Paper I but employed a Brus-sels extended Skyrme interaction [ 28 ]. Additional momentum-dependent terms allow for a more complex density dependence arXiv:2607.09369v1 [nucl-th] 10 Jul 2026 2of meff and provide greater flexibility for the EDF. This pre-vents meff from decreasing excessively, which is a necessary condition for vF;n/c < 1 and can even produce a U-shaped den-sity dependence, qualitatively agreeing with ab initio models featuring three body forces [ 29 –31 ]. The density dependence of meff is of particular interest for numerical simulations of core-collapse supernovae and binary neutron star mergers be-cause it determines the thermal response of matter [ 32 , 33 ]. Models allowing meff to increase with density are notewor-thy because they accommodate negative thermal pressure over wide domains of temperature, density, and proton fraction [ 34 ]. The increased flexibility of the EDF allows NEPs and global parameters of NSs to span broader domains and erases spurious correlations among NEPs or between NEPs and parameters of NSs. Paper II showed that in most cases Esym increases with density. The configuration included 7 tempered chains with 1 000 walkers each with maximum tem-perature T = 16384 and automatic temperature adjustments for intermediate chains. > IV. RESULTS

The following subsections discuss the properties of NM in terms of mPNM eff ; n , mSNM eff ; N , energy per particle ( E/A) in SNM and PNM, and Easym as functions of density, alongside marginal-ized posterior distributions of NEPs, E/A in PNM, mPNM eff ; n , and 

mSNM eff ; N . NS properties are discussed via the density dependence of the speed of sound squared and proton fraction as well as marginalized posterior distributions of key global observables. We also evaluate compliance with astrophysical inferences on the radii of 1 .4 M⊙ and 2 M⊙ NSs and the tidal deformability of 1.4 M⊙ NSs, which were not included in the likelihood. Special attention is given to the correlations between NEPs and NS parameters, as well as comparisons with existing literature. > D. Thermal response

Following Refs. [ 32 – 34 , 53 ], we investigate the finite-temperature behavior of NM in terms of eth and pth as functions of density for fixed values of Yp and temperature. In addition to EoSs generated in this work and EoSs in 11 F, we also show the behavior of BBSk6, BBSk7 and BBSk8. The latter e ff ective interactions were generated in Paper II. General purpose EoS tables built for these interactions according to Ref. [ 54 ] are publicly available on C omp OSE [ 55 ] online repository 3. For their characterization in terms of NEPs and values of global NS properties, see Table A. Fig. 7 considers the behavior of eth for Yp = 0, 0.2, and 0.5 and T = 5 and 20 MeV. In all considered cases, eth in-creases with density and temperature. In most cases, for a given temperature and density, higher values of Yp lead to higher values of eth . However, this is not a universal rule, see, e.g., BBSk7. BBSk7 has lower values of meff ;n/p compared to those of BBSk6 and BBSk8 for Yp = 0.2 and 0.5; also, for BBSk7, meff ;n/p(n) has the least pronounced U-shape (not shown). Finally, the EoSs generated in this work behave sim-ilarly to those in the 11 F set. This was expected based on Eq. (2.11) and the similarity between the e ff ective masses for these to sets of models; see Fig. 1. Fig. 8 investigates the behavior of pth (n) for the temperatures and proton fractions considered in Fig. 7. For n ≲ 3nsat , the dispersion among the models is weak and in all situations 

pth increases with the density. For n ≳ 3nsat , both the EoSs generated here and those in 11 F allow for a wide range of behaviors. For Yp = 0, more (less) than half of the models built here (in 11 F) predict that pth increases with density. For 

Yp = 0.2 and 0.5, all EoSs exhibit a non-monotonic “rise-and-fall” behavior of pth (n). The same holds true for Yp =

0.02 (not shown). The least (most) pronounced rise-and-fall behavior of pth (n) corresponds to BBSk6 (BBSk8), which has the least (most) pronounced U-shaped density dependence of 

meff ;i. A significant number of models predicts that, at high densities, pth < 0. As explained at length in Ref. [ 34 ], this is a consequence of the U-shaped meff ;i(n). The di ff erences between the quantiles (median, upper, and lower) of the models developed in this work and those in 11 F are more pronounced than those in Fig. 7, because, unlike eth , pth depends also on 

∂meff ;i/∂ n (see Eq. (2.12)).  

> V. CONCLUSION

In this work, we have extended and generalized the studies presented in Paper II concerning the ability of non-relativistic models with Brussels extended Skyrme interactions to describe matter at densities exceeding several times nsat and isospin  

> 3https://compose.obspm.fr/

asymmetries 0 ≤ δ ≤ 1. To fully utilize the flexibility of the EDF, all 13 parameters of the e ff ective interaction were allowed to vary. Then, to ensure physical consistency across the density range relevant for NSs (assuming only nucleonic degrees of freedom), “sanity” conditions on NS and PNM EoSs were imposed up to the central density of the maximum mass configuration ( n∗ 

> c

≈ 1.0 − 1.2 fm −3). These conditions in-cluded thermodynamic stability, causality, neutron and proton eff ective masses and Fermi velocities. The parameter space was explored via MCMC procedure, with models weighted based on their agreement with fiducial values for the four best-known NEPs ( nsat , Esat , Ksat and Jsym ), energy per particle and neutron e ff ective mass in PNM with 0 .08 ≤ n [fm −3] ≤ 0.16 as computed by χEFT, neutron e ff ective mass in SNM with 0.08 ≤ n [fm −3] ≤ 0.16 as computed by χEFT, the requirement that M∗ 

> G

> 2 M⊙ and the electron direct Urca threshold mass 

MDU ≥ 1.5 M⊙.The tighter constraints employed in this work, compared to those in Paper II, filter out a large number of models with extreme behaviors of mPNM eff ; n (n), mSNM eff ; N (n), and Easym (n) lead-ing to significant modifications of the marginalized posterior distributions of all NS global parameters. NS EoSs become sti ff er, leading to larger values of R1.4, Λ1.4, I1.4, R2.0, Λ2.0,and I2.0 with respect to Paper II. While the extra flexibility of the 13-parameter EDF further sti ff ens the EoS, agreement with constraints from Refs. [ 5] and [ 12 ] remains modest, likely because the flexibility of the phenomenological EDF is still limited. It is interesting to notice that, with the exception of 

Lsym and Ksym , the NEPs are unable to capture the way in which the behavior of NM is modified when passing from 11 to 13 e ff ective interaction parameters. For all the models built here, mSNM eff ; N (n) is U-shaped, and in most cases, mPNM eff ; n (n) is also U-shaped. These features result in pronounced “rise-all-fall” behaviors of pth (n) in NM with 

Yp ≳ 0.01 and negative thermal pressures at high densities. We expect that these EoSs, when implemented in numerical simulations of CCSN and BNS mergers, will produce signa-tures distinct from those obtained using nucleonic models with standard Skyrme interactions. Alongside other studies in the literature, e.g., Refs. [ 14 –17 ], which employ non-relativistic mean-field models, and Refs. [ 15 , 18 –24 ], which employ relativistic mean-field models, this work aims to determine the extent to which phenomeno-logical models are adequate for describing dense matter and to which present and future constraints from multi-messenger astronomy of NSs can improve our understanding of NM.
