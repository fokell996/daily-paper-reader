We study tides during the inspiral of a binary neutron star system, including nonlinear hydrody-namical interactions. Using an affine approximation that treats the perturbed neutron star as an ellipsoid, we analytically derive coupling coefficients among the quadrupolar f-modes and the radial mode to the four-wave order (i.e., next-to-next-to-leading order) in the Hamiltonian, allowing for arbitrary (aligned or anti-aligned) rotation of the background star. Our model reveals a series of universal relations from first-principles arguments. Besides the well-known relations, such as the one between tidal deformability and moment of inertia, we show that the three-wave (next-to-leading-order) interaction coefficients are fully determined by the properties of the linear tide. Therefore, they do not probe new physics of the neutron star. Nonetheless, not including the three-wave non-linear tides can lead to significant systematic errors in the gravitational waveform. We support this claim via a hybrid approach that simultaneously captures mode resonances expected in Newtonian hydrodynamics and is consistent with relativistic calculations in the low-frequency expansion. The nonlinear tide in a single neutron star can cause a phase shift of around 1.7 radians accumulated up to merger compared to the linear tide model; for a binary of similar masses, the phase shift is approximately doubled. Our calculation extends to four-wave interactions, which, for a slowly spinning neutron star, provide only small corrections and are subdominant compared to the tidal back-reaction on the orbit. For a rapidly rotating neutron star, the nonlinear centrifugal drive of the f-mode and the four-wave anharmonicity provides a window to study the adiabatic exponent related to internal neutron star buoyancy that cannot be probed by the linear and three-wave tides in slowly spinning systems. The anharmonicity cannot lead to resonance locking of the f-mode. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Tidal interactions in a binary neutron star (BNS) or a neutron star-black hole (NSBH) system inspiralling due to gravitational waves (GWs) offer a valuable window to simultaneously study the neutron star (NS) equation of state at extreme densities [1] and the dynamics at extreme spacetime curvatures [2]. So far, theoretical studies of BNS tidal interactions have been treating the tide as a small perturbation to the background star (e.g., [3–28]). In this case, the NS’s response has been calculated only to the linear order in the perturbed displacement over the background radius in the fluid’s equation of motion, or quadratic order in the potential energies (or the Lagrangian or Hamiltonian). This linear theory works well in the early inspiral when the binary components are far apart. However, as the orbit decays to smaller separations, the tidal bulge increases to a significant fraction of the unperturbed radius. Moreover, the tidal forcing frequency approaches the frequency of an NS eigenmode in the late inspiral, significantly amplifying the tidal bulge while creating a lag in it via mode resonance. Nonlinear hydrodynamical effects are thus expected to become significant. The authors of [29] studied large-scale nonlinear tides (i.e., nonlinear interactions among fundamental f-modes) in BNSs within Newtonian hydrodynamics. They showed that the nonlinear tide can cause a GW phase shift of O(1) rad.

2With conservative assumptions, [30] showed that ignoring the nonlinear tide can cause a bias of at least 8% in the inferred NS tidal deformability. Recently, [31] extended the calculation of [29] to a relativistic NS, and [32] provided the associated GW waveform, confirming the significance of nonlinear tides in general relativity (GR). However, both studies were based on low-frequency or even adiabatic approximations where the tidal forcing frequency is assumed to be much lower than the f-mode frequency, with an expansion in their ratio made. However, [29] showed that the dominant nonlinear correction is, in fact, a decrease in the f-mode eigenfrequency, which becomes most significant during the late inspiral, when the orbit approaches resonance with the f-mode, and the tidal response is sensitive to small perturbations to the frequency. Therefore, a low-frequency expansion likely underestimates the impact of the nonlinear tide by an appreciable amount. In this work, we will apply a hybrid approach where we evolve the hydrodynamical equations of [29] that capture all dynamical effects expected in Newtonian theory (e.g., mode resonance), while applying a calibration procedure to the coefficients such that under a low-frequency expansion, our result is consistent with the GR calculation of [31]. While not exactly rigorous in full GR (but at least the functional forms are exact in the Newtonian limit), it may provide a theoretically motivated estimate of the impact of the nonlinear tide beyond the low-frequency limit. To compute the nonlinear coupling coefficients analytically and allow an arbitrary value of the spin of the background star, we will adopt the affine model proposed by [33, 34]. This model was used to study the inspiral of BNSs and NSBHs in the Newtonian limit by [35–37] and in post-Newtonian (PN) theory by [38, 39]; these studies used fluid variables (e.g., the major axes of the ellipsoids) that did not make the hydrodynamical nature explicit. [40] recently revisited the affine model and instead linked the fluid variables to amplitudes of the NS monopolar (with a polar degree la = 0) radial mode and quadrupolar ( la = 2) f-modes. This representation made the appearance of the f-mode resonance transparent, while simultaneously offering an intuitive interpretation of the dominant nonlinear hydrodynamical effects as corrections to the natural frequencies of the modes [29]. Nonetheless, in the low-frequency limit, corrections to the coupling to the external field enter at the same order as the frequency shift. Thus, when resumming the low-frequency expansion into the Lorentzian form expected for a forced harmonic oscillator, as attempted by [31], these two effects must be separated; at least for the particular solution, or equivalently, the equilibrium tide in the convention of [22]). The approach of [40] further provides a direct way to extract various nonlinear coupling coefficients [41–46] among the f- and radial modes. In this work, we will follow [40] to study the nonlinear tides in an NS during its inspiral with another compact object companion. The affine approximation of [34, 40] contains only a finite number of internal NS parameters (two in a Newtonian model, the polytropic and adiabatic exponents, Γ and Γ ad , respectively; an additional compactness in a relativistic one). Consequently, various linear and nonlinear coefficients derived from it will be related, revealing a series of universal relations from first-principles calculations (similar to [47–49]). In addition to the well-known relations, such as the tidal deformability–moment of inertia [50] and the tidal deformability–f-mode frequency relations [47] (see [51] for a review), the affine model further connects three-wave couplings (i.e., the next-to-leading-order, or NLO tidal effect) to the properties of the linear tide, which are ultimately determined by only the polytropic exponent and compactness of the NS. This connection has important implications for GW data analysis. Specifically, it means that the parameters required to describe the NLO nonlinear tides in a slowly spinning system do not represent fundamentally new properties of the NS, and therefore can be expressed in terms of the linear tidal deformability, thereby reducing the dimensionality of the parameter space explored during inference. On the other hand, ignoring the nonlinear tide (as nearly all current BNS and NSBH waveforms do) will lead to biases in the inferred deformability. Such biases will be significant in the era of third-generation GW detectors (including the Einstein Telescope [52, 53] and the Cosmic Explorer [54–56]), where both a large number of total detections and loud individual events are expected, as recently investigated by [30]. Note that the affine model considered in this work is a one-mode approximation that considers only the tidal coupling to the f-modes. It cannot capture smaller-scale perturbations caused by, e.g., the gravity modes (g-modes), whose nonlinear effects may also be significant. For example, a low-order (long-wavelength) g-mode could experience resonance locking due to the conservative anharmonicity [57, 58], whereas a high-order g-mode (short-wavelength) might lead to significant fluid dissipation via the pg-instability [45, 46, 59–61]. We also ignore inertial [62–65] and interface modes [66–68] in this study. Throughout this analysis, we adopt geometric units, setting G = c = 1. Einstein summation is assumed, with Latin indices i, j, ..., running from 1 to 3 denoting the three spatial components expressed in a Cartesian coordinate system and used interchangeably in superscripts and subscripts. However, summation over NS eigenmodes will always be made explicit. The eigenmodes will be labeled by subscripts a, b, . . . . For a particular mode, its label a will be replaced either by its azimuthal quantum number ma (when referring to the quadrupolar f-modes) or a label r (when referring to the monopolar, or radial mode). We use subscripts A and B to label the tidally perturbed star and the companion, respectively. By default, we will use MA = MB = 1 .35 M⊙ and RA = 11 .7 km, with a compactness MA/R A = 0 .17. 6) No resonance locking of the f-mode and a new probe of Γad . While our analytical solutions focus on the equilibrium tides inside slowly-spinning NSs, our formulation allows for an arbitrary spin rate. Two sets of numerical experiments of rapidly spinning NSs with f-mode resonantly excited during the inspiral are conducted in Secs. III B and III C. In the first experiment, we demonstrate that the f-mode does not experience resonance locking. An approximately linear-in-frequency increase in the tidal spin observed by [72] after resonance can already be reproduced by linear theory due to the overshooting of a second-order system, with nonlinear tide introducing corrections only at a quantitative level (see the lower panel of Fig. 5). In the second experiment, we demonstrate the dependence on Γ ad through the nonlinear centrifugal drive and NNLO shift of the f-mode frequency. This parameter is not probed by the linear and NLO tides in a slowly spinning NS. When the f-mode is resonantly excited, ∼ 1 rad change in the GW phase is observed as Γ ad changes (Fig. 6). Effectively, this means that the nonlinear tide involves aspects associated with the composition of the high-density nuclear matter.

> A. Theoretical universal relations

Numerous universal relations between different NS properties have been observed (see, e.g., [51] for a review), and multiple authors have examined the theoretical origin behind those relations [47–49], which mainly relies on the fact that fluid is nearly incompressible (hence the affine approximation works well). We present derivations of some well-known universal relations and provide an intuitive argument for their existence based on counting the number of free parameters of the affine model. Adding to the literature, our formulation reveals new universal relations connecting the nonlinear tidal properties to the linear ones. In the affine approximation of [33, 34], various energies (hence the fluid dynamics) are fully specified by only three parameters, ( M∗, Γ, Γad ). They can be viewed as representing three degrees of freedom in the problem: a theory of gravity governing the hydrostatic equilibrium, an equation of state governing the equilibrium pressure-density relation, and the P − ρ relation in an “adiabatic” (which can also mean constant composition in an NS) process. For a fixed theory of gravity (e.g., Newtonian or GR), we further have M∗ = M∗(Γ) despite the lack of a closed-form expression in general. In other words, the presence of various universal relations in NSs is not a surprise; they are natural consequences of the fact that there are only two intrinsic parameters, (Γ , Γad ), describing a large-scale perturbation. Practically, we will use the Love number k2A (or the deformability λA) instead of Γ to represent the first parameter. All the linear (leading-order) and three-wave (NLO) tidal interactions are fully determined by it. The other parameter, Γad can be probed only at the four-wave level (NNLO). > III. EQUILIBRIUM SOLUTIONS OF THE HYDRODYNAMICS

In this section, we describe the solutions to the internal hydrodynamics in terms of mode amplitudes. They will then be used in the next section to derive the tidal back-reactions to the orbit, and consequently, the final tidal corrections to the GW phase. Our analytical solutions (Sec. III A) will focus on the “equilibrium tide” following the convention of [22], defined as the component in ca that has a phase dependence of exp( −im aϕ) (i.e., the particular solution of a driven harmonic oscillator). It contains finite-frequency corrections in the form of a Lorentzian. In contrast, the dynamical tide is the component that varies as exp[ −iω a0t − im a(1 + Ca)Ω At] (the homogeneous solution excited at mode resonance), and its impact will only be numerically investigated in Secs. III B and III C. All numerical integrations start at r = 8 RA, using the analytical equilibrium tide solutions as the initial conditions, and terminate at r = 2 RA. For the mode amplitude evolutions presented in this section, a Γ = 1+1 /n = 2 polytrope will be assumed (either Newtonian or relativistic), whereas our discussion on tidal dephasing in Sec. IV will cover both a polytropic Γ = 2 (very soft) and the SLy [70] equation of state (medium-soft). In most cases, we take Γ ad = 2, which has little impact on the result as it does not enter at the three-wave (NLO) dynamics for a slowly-spinning NS as discussed in Sec. II A, except for in Sec. III C where we will discuss how Γ ad may be probed in a rapidly spinning NS.

> V. DISCUSSION

The main results of the paper were summarized in Sec. I A. We highlight again that the nonlinear tide from a single SLy NS can cause a GW phase shift of ≃ 1.7 rad at merger than predicted by the linear theory. The effect exists in all BNSs and NSBHs to be detected, and therefore, can have a significant impact on the inferred NS equation of state through stacking a population of detections [94, 95]. The presence of slow spins, however, can potentially complicate the problem. In the rare case where a rapidly spinning NS is detected with f-mode resonantly excited, we also showed that such a system offers a good opportunity to probe new NS physics, Γ ad , which can hardly be probed through f-modes (but potentially through g-modes [57, 89, 96]) in slowly spinning systems. Our study also suggests multiple directions for future studies. As the affine model is a powerful tool to study the nonlinear tide analytically, it is interesting to upgrade our Newtonian model to a relativistic one, or at least include PN corrections as done in [38, 39]. This may theoretically explain why the scalings introduced in Sec. II B work well in GR. Such a calculation would also provide the relativistic values of κ2If and J2 separately, as they enter ∆ ω2 (line 84a) and ∆ V2 (line 85a) in different combinations than p2A (Eq. 62). This separation is needed for NLO beyond the low-frequency limit. Currently, we assume that they scale the same way in GR based on their Newtonian universal relations with ω2  

> f0

(Sec. II C), although the validity of this assumption should be assessed more carefully. Since our study focused primarily on the tidal response, we adopted a Newtonian description of the orbit with quadrupolar GW radiation. Formally, the resulting error in the GW waveform is subleading in both tidal and PN effects. In practice, however, tidal effects can become sizable during the late inspiral, requiring a more sophisticated treatment of the orbital dynamics to produce faithful GW waveforms. For example, the Hamiltonian developed in this work can be readily resummed into an EOB form [74] to incorporate relativistic orbital dynamics, similar to the approach of [25]. 34 We remind the reader that some theoretical uncertainties arise from an unknown 3PN boundary term, which causes the GR values of ¨k2 ∝ 1/ω 2  

> f0

to differ between [20] and [23] (see Fig. 1). We adopted the results of [20] in this work, as they provide a more conservative estimate of the tidal effects. The same uncertainty may also affect the relativistic value of p2A and, consequently, Eqs. (67) and (68). The issue may be resolved by comparing the to-be-developed EOB waveform with nonlinear tides to numerical relativity simulations to fit for the values of ¨k2A and p2A.For data-analysis purposes, computationally efficient phenomenological models are essential. As in [32], our Eq. (146) can be treated as an additive correction to any baseline binary-black-hole waveform. When doing so, one should first compute the full analytical solution for the mode amplitudes (Sec. III A) and then evaluate ∆ E and ∆ ˙E in terms of these amplitudes. The low-frequency expansion, Eq. (147), significantly underestimates the tidal corrections. In developing such waveform models, two issues must be addressed. First, a more sophisticated treatment is required to compute γ2d, accounting for the tidal contributions to ˙ r and ˙ ω, which dominate over the point-particle predictions in regimes where tidal effects become important. Second, one should account for the fact that different waveforms generally have different durations in the time domain, an effect not sufficiently captured by ∆Ψ in Eq. (133) or the left panels of Figs. 9 and 10. Since ∆Ψ( f ) ≃ − ∆ϕgw (t) (eq. 94 of [22]), the issue is better illustrated in the time-domain phase shown in the right panels of Fig. 9 and 10. In addition to measuring the phase shift at a given time, the phase shift at a given orbital separation (reached at different times) also has physical significance, as the location of the merger is more naturally defined by an orbital separation. At times when only one waveform is defined, phase shift may not be a sufficient description.
