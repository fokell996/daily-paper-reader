Abstract 

Previous studies have shown that δ Scuti stars can be used for determining spacecraft position and time similar to X-ray pulsar navigation, but open ques-tions remain regarding the light-curve stability, and, therefore, the navigation accuracy that can be derived from δ Scuti variable stars. Here, we develop a com-putational framework to identify δ Scuti variable stars with light curves that are suitable for spacecraft navigation purposes. Our approach emphasizes quantify-ing timing uncertainty through developing metrics and evaluating such metrics in the context of spacecraft navigation. We analyze over 110 δ Scuti variable stars from the Kepler space telescope and 10 additional stars from the K2 mission. For each star, we produce a simple model of its normalized flux as a function of time, along with several metrics used to assess its suitability for navigation. Model quality was further assessed through comparing predictions with obser-vations from the Transiting Exoplanet Survey Satellite (TESS). Out of the 120 

δ Scuti variable stars we investigated in this study, 32 stars were identified as candidates predictable enough to enable spacecraft navigation. 

Keywords: δ Scuti variable stars, Deep space probes, Multi-periodic pulsation, Autonomous spacecraft navigation, Computational astronomy, Space systems

# 1 Introduction 

Current limitations in interplanetary communications have enhanced the demand for spacecraft autonomy including the capability of recovering from extended loss of power and/or communication with the Earth during deep space missions [1–3]. Previous studies have shown that autonomous optical observations of δ Scuti variable stars with readily available on-board systems can be used in combination with other optical Celestial Navigation techniques to recover from extended navigation blackouts [4]. The brightness variation of δ Scuti stars can be used to generate initial estimates of space-craft position and time even if prior state knowledge is limited to ”somewhere within the solar system”. Stable light-curve predictions of variable stars are a prerequisite for this method. 

δ Scuti stars, however, often exhibit multi-modal pulsation as well as long-term frequency or amplitude variations which complicates efforts to model their light curves accurately. Few methods currently exist that can reliably predict the variability of δ

Scuti variable stars. A previous study by Bowman et al. (2016) showed that the pre-diction stability of the light-curve of these stars can be assessed by segmenting the light curve, computing amplitude spectra for each segment, and evaluating consistency of the amplitude spectra [5]. An alternative approach by Breger (2016) involves con-ducting a Fourier analysis with pre-whitening of the light curve to identify pulsation modes, whose amplitudes and frequencies are then tracked across multiple segments of the light curve to assess variability [6]. Similar to previous approaches we model the pulsation of δ Scuti stars as super-positions of periodic functions. In contrast to previous work, we do not necessarily aim for the highest possible fidelity in our δ Scuti models - instead we propose sev-eral metrics computed in the time and frequency domains to assess the predictability 

of certain modes of δ Scuti variable stars in the framework of spacecraft navigation. By applying these metrics to δ Scutis observed by the Kepler space telescope [7, 8] we determine a set of stars that we deem suitable for use in spacecraft navigation. To this end we have developed a systematic framework to analyze a large data set of δ Scuti variable stars efficiently. The methodology presented in this work is generalizable and can be extended to variable stars beyond the sample studied here. The remaining article is structured as follows. In sections 2 and 3 we provide a brief introduction to δ Scuti stars and discuss their pulsation modes and relevant models. Section 4 describes the methodology we use to generate simplified models and identify suitable candidate stars for spacecraft navigation. In sections 5 and 6 we discuss results of our modeling efforts and deliver some insights in the spacecraft navigation performance we expect when using the proposed set of δ Scuti stars. The code base used in this work is publicly available via GitHub [9]. 

# 2 What are δ Scuti Variable Stars? 

Variable stars change in brightness over time due to either intrinsic mechanisms, such as pulsation, or extrinsic reasons, such as eclipses in binary systems [10]. δ Scuti stars in particular are variable stars of spectral types A to F that lie in the instability strip 2in the main sequence of the Hertzsprung Russell Diagram [11]. These stars exhibit rel-atively short pulsation periods when compared to other variable stars such as Classical Cepheids and RR Lyrae stars [12]. Oscillations in the brightness of δ Scuti variable stars, typically with pulsation periods of 18 minutes to 8 hours, are linked to volumetric expansion and contraction of the star [13]. δ Scuti variable stars typically have an effective temperature range of 6300 ≤ Teff ≤ 8600 K and stars with higher temperature typically have shorter pulsation periods than stars with lower temperatures [5]. There are two different modes of pulsation for δ Scuti variable stars: radial and non-radial modes. Pulsations that are non-radial feature some parts of the surface of a star contracting inwards and other parts expanding outward simultaneously. In contrast, radial pulsations are less common and behave differently. Stars with radial pulsation expand and contract in their equilibrium state and alter only the radius of the δ Scuti variable star to maintain its spherical shape [14]. The pulsation behavior observed in δ Scuti variable stars likely stems from the stars’ κ-mechanism operating in the helium-rich atmosphere of these stars [15]. To sus-tain a star’s pulsation over time, continuous energy input into the system is required. The oscillations in brightness of these stars are, therefore, associated with an oscillation in the distribution of the stars internal energy [16]. With variation in internal energy, the local helium density within a δ Scuti variable star also oscillates. During the compression phase of the pulsation cycle, helium is compressed to become more ionized and opaque [17]. The compressed and denser helium blocks outward flow of radiation and as a result, the amount of light being blocked from escaping continuously increases until the dimmest part of the star’s cycle. The light, now blocked from escaping, instead builds up within the star and increases the local temperature. The helium heats and expands, decreasing the opacity of the star and light once again escapes more easily. The star eventually returns to its expanded state and allows for gravity to once again pull the δ Scuti variable star’s outer layers inward and restart the cycle [18]. This cycle repeats it self throughout the stellar evolution of the δ Scuti variable star and as a result the pulsation of the star is observed [14]. 

# 3 Predictive Models for the Variability of δ Scuti Variable Stars 

Beyond their astrophysical significance, δ Scuti variable stars have also been proposed for use in autonomous spacecraft navigation: the brightness variation of δ Scuti stars can be used to generate an initial estimate spacecraft position and time with very limited prior knowledge [4]. This method relies on predictable light-curves of variable stars. Previous mode stability analysis of δ Scuti variable stars have suggested that they may exhibit stable pulsation modes over a extended period of time [5, 19, 20]. While this implies that the oscillatory behavior of δ Scuti variable stars may be stable, their variability still presents significant challenges for predictive modeling. Factors such as multi-mode pulsations, amplitude modulation, and long-term frequency variations 3complicate efforts to accurately predict and generate models for the pulsation behavior of these stars [21, 22]. Furthermore, high-precision photometric observations indicate that the light curves of δ Scuti stars deviate from a single frequency sinusoidal model and instead exhibit multi-frequency oscillation. Consequently, the generation of an accurate predictive model for δ Scuti variable stars is further complicated [14]. The complexity of the pulsation of δ Scuti variable stars is further amplified when devel-oping a generalized framework that is capable of handling sets of δ Scuti variable stars exhibiting resonant mode coupling and non-linear interactions between pulsation modes [13]. Despite the challenges in predicting the oscillatory behavior of δ Scuti variable stars, we present a computational framework designed to produce predictive models and by extension identify a set of δ Scuti variable stars that exhibit consistent and stable pulsation behavior. 

# 4 A Simple Model for δ Scutis 

In 2006, NASA launched the Kepler space telescope that surveyed a single fixed patch of sky in the constellations of Cygnus and Lyra, with the primary goal of identifying exoplanets – small planets that could sustain life beyond Earth – and more impor-tantly, collecting high-precision photometric data [7]. While its main objective was to identify exoplanets, it was also able to observe over 500,000 stars at high cadence, enabling a wide range of studies on variable stars. Among those were a significant number of δ Scuti variable stars. Each observed star was assigned a unique iden-tifier (KIC) in the Kepler input catalog [23]. Data collected by the Kepler mission includes measurements of periodicity, flux, variability as well as spatial coordinates [24]. Kepler data is particularly valuable due to its high cadence, long duration, and nearly continuous photometric coverage. In this work we use Fourier synthesis as a basis for fitting and predicting δ Scuti variable star light curves. Stellar pulsation modes are often represented using complex notation, e.g., Ce iωt , where ω is the angular frequency of the pulsation mode and C is a complex coefficient encoding both amplitude and phase. B cos( ωt ), where A and B are complex-valued amplitudes. Since the observed light curve is strictly real valued, we will represent light curves as a single sinusoid with a phase offset, ϕi, in the form Ai sin(2 πf it + ϕi). Due to the multi-modal pulsation behavior of δ Scuti variable stars effective multi-modal modeling must be considered [27]. We begin with identifying all significant pulsation modes for our δ Scuti sample. Each pulsation mode is characterized by a unique frequency, indicating its oscillation rate, and a corresponding amplitude nor-malization scalar, Ω, reflecting the dominance of a particular mode in the variability of the stellar light curve. To obtain these modes, we produce the normalized peri-odogram of each δ Scuti variable star, with each significant local maximum of the frequency equal to the frequency of the pulsation mode and the amplitude equal to the normalization of the amplitude of the pulsation mode. In Figure 2, the periodogram of KIC 3429637 identifies three dominant pulsation modes, of which the one with the highest amplitude exhibits a frequency of 10.33 cycles/day. To ensure that only easily detectable pulsation modes are included in our predictive model, normalized ampli-tudes below 0.1 were excluded. This filtering step helps avoid overfitting and reduces the number of terms in a star’s composite pulsation model. We fit the model presented in Equation (1) to each significant pulsation mode in the light curve of a δ Scuti star using a least squares approach. The initial amplitude of the pulsation mode was set as the range between the 95 th and 50 th percentile normalized flux of the star’s light curve. NRMSE is a scalar quantity, ranging from 0 to ∞, which indicates how well the model predicts the observed light curve of a δ Scuti variable star. A lower NRMSE value indicates small deviation between the model and the light curve of the δ Scuti variable star, indicating a high accuracy fit. Inversely, a higher NRMSE value reflects large divergence, suggesting that the model fails to capture the variability of the star. A model that perfectly captures the light curve of the star would produce a NRMSE value of 0. Models designed for stars with non-linear pulsation modes, amplitude modulations, or long term frequency variations tend to yield greater NRMSE values. This indicates that the model has a poor fit and, as a result, a limited ability to accurately predict the light curve of the star. By applying a consistent NRMSE value threshold across a population of δ Scuti variables, stars that are more predictable can be identified. Stars that yielded greater NRMSE values typically showed greater divergence between the model and observed light curve. While the NRMSE stability threshold will vary between data sets and applications, in our analysis stars with NRMSE values below 0.21, approximately one standard deviation of the NRMSE distribution, were considered predictable. Those stars, comprising approximately 29% of the the total population of stars analyzed, rep-resent the most predictable candidates and may be suitable for navigation applications such as autonomous spacecraft navigation [4]. However, stars with higher NRMSE values do not necessarily have to be excluded. Higher NRMSE values merely indicate that further study of these stars is required to better understand and model their variability. # 6.2 Navigation Performance 

Monte Carlo simulations are conducted to simulate navigation performance. In [4], the authors fit pulsation models to synthetic light curves in order to estimate ∆ t. The synthetic light curves do not contain systematics and other perturbations that could appear in a real light curve, so the estimate of ∆ t may be better than practically achievable, leading to an overoptimistic assessment of navigation performance. In this study, we estimate ∆ t by fitting our pulsation model to real light curves from TESS/Kepler/K2 data products to mitigate this shortcoming. Each δ Scuti star’s pulsation model is fitted to a randomly chosen 1-day light curve segment from the available data to estimate ∆ t. Furthermore, Gaussian noise is added to the light curve before fitting to reduce the effective photometric precision of the light curve to levels comparable to that of MapCam onboard the OSIRIS-APEX spacecraft [37]. 22 7 Conclusion 

This study presents a generalized framework for evaluating the predictability of δ Scuti vari-able stars through developing predictive models of their light curves as superpositions of sinusoidal functions suitable for fast and efficient on-board processing. The models were con-structed from Kepler data and compared to observations from TESS. Model accuracy was assessed through multiple metrics to identify stars capable of being accurately predicted and modeled. We used the normalized root-mean-squre-error (NRMSE) to quantify the average size of prediction error between the model and the star’s light curve relative to its brightness. In the frequency domain, R2 

> LSP

, was used to measure how well the model captured all dom-inant pulsation modes of the star. The stability of pulsations modes was evaluated through computing an additional R2 

> LSP

metric between the Kepler derived models and TESS data. Furthermore, we introduced ϵ, which quantified the timing errors. We also tested for long term consistency of the models to understand how long the models presented in this article will be valid for. Out of the 110 δ Scuti stars investigated in this work, we identified the 32 most stable ones. We then used the models of 8 δ Scuti variable stars developed from Kepler data together with models of 10 K2 δ Scuti variable stars beyond the Kepler field as input for the ”cold-start” spacecraft navigation method presented in [4]. We find that the achievable position and time estimates are slightly less accurate than previous estimates suggested, which is largely due to more realistic noise in actual light curve data. We also show that choosing δ Scuti stars that are well distributed on the celestial sphere enhances the fidelity of spacecraft navigation applications and mitigates the dilution of precision as outlined in Appendix Appendix D:. An extension of this framework to more δ Scuti variables may provide deeper insight on the population of stable variable stars and improve the precision of light curve predictive models. A significant constraint is the limited availability of high-cadence photometric data, which is essential for accurately modeling light curves of δ Scuti variable stars. Future mis-sions designed to collect high cadence photometric data, such as the Planetary Transits and Oscillations of Stars (PLATO) mission scheduled to launch in December 2026, will open the opportunity to extend this study to a broader and more spatially distributed set of δ Scuti variable stars [40]. All code used to generate the results described in this paper is publicly available on GitHub repository [9].

62 Appendix D: Time and Position Dilution of Precision 

Dilution of precision is typically illustrated in two physical dimensions by intersecting the error bounds of signals from two sources. If those sources have nearly orthogonal lines of sight to the observer, the intersection region (i.e. uncertainty or geometric dilution of precision) is small. If the lines of sight are close together, the signals will be nearly parallel and result in a large intersection region. A common example is shown in Figure D.1.  

> Fig. D.1 Geometric dilution of precision as illustrated in [46].

A potential misconception is that dilution of precision will result in the greatest uncer-tainty in directions orthogonal to the line of sight to the signal sources [47, 48]. Although this may be true if only physical dimensions are considered, it is not necessarily the case when time or clock error is also being estimated (as is the case in this study and commonly in global satellite navigation systems). Signals from nearly parallel sources intersect over a longer period of time compared to signals from disparate directions, resulting in greater time dilution of precision and geometric dilution of precision. In Figure D.2, the left column shows three signals (gray bars) traveling approximately from right to left, with their direction of travel rotated by 15 ◦ each. The measurement timing uncertainty is represented by the thickness of the bars. The position uncertainty of the observer is then the overlap of the three signals, which is shown in red. The signals travel over time and their intersection geometry changes. The combined intersections, representing observer position uncertainty across all time uncertainties, is shown at the bottom of the figure. The direction of greatest position uncertainty is aligned with the signals’ direction of travel. The right column shows the same analysis but for three signals traveling horizontally, vertically, and diagonally. The total position uncertainty is much smaller. In the context of navigation with δ Scuti stars, the seemingly contradictory conclusion is that the direction in which light curves are being measured is also the direction which has the greatest position uncertainty. This is evident in the navigation simulation results from Figure 10.
