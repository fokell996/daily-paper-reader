## Hyperonic equation of state for neutron stars: A systematic Bayesian comparison of density-dependent and non-linear relativistic mean-field models

(Dated: June 23, 2026) A systematic Bayesian inference study of the equation of state (EOS) of dense matter with strangeness is presented extending five relativistic mean-field (RMF) models with both constant and density dependent couplings to include the full baryon octet. The hyperon-nucleon couplings in the scalar channel are varied within ranges informed by hypernuclear data, while vector isoscalar couplings are fixed by the SU(6) symmetry quark model. Observational constraints from NICER (PSR J0030, J0437, J0740) and GW170817, theoretical constraints from chiral effective field theory (χEFT) and perturbative QCD (pQCD), and experimental constraints from nuclear saturation properties are imposed simultaneously. We find that the inclusion of hyperons systematically reduces the maximum neutron star mass by 0 .05–0 .10 M⊙ across all models while increasing the radius at 1.4 M⊙ by 0 .5–0 .8 km. The speed of sound exhibits a characteristic softening at densities 2–3 ρ0

coinciding with hyperon onset. All hyperonic models remain consistent with the 2 M⊙ constraint. Models with a more flexible isovector channel span a larger proton fraction when only nucleons are included. However, the extra flexibility is visibly suppressed by hyperons, meaning that the average proton distribution is independent of model flexibility when hyperons are included. Less flexible models show comparable or slightly increased proton fractions due to EOS stiffening when hyperons are included. Only a residual number of hyperonic EOS give rise to a mass-radius curve with a negative slope at low masses. A 1 .8M⊙ neutron star with a radius larger than or similar to the radius of a 1 .2M⊙ star would give a strong indication that the star contains other baryonic degrees of freedom in addition to nucleons. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Neutron stars (NSs), which are remnants of massive stellar explosions, are among the most extreme objects in the Universe. The equation of state (EOS) of dense matter is a central ingredient in modeling their structure and observable properties such as mass, radius, and tidal deformability [1–3]. While purely nucleonic models have been extensively constrained by multi-messenger astro-physical observations, the appearance of heavier baryons – hyperons (Λ , Σ, Ξ) – inside neutron stars is still de-bated. The onset of hyperons at densities of 2–3 times nuclear saturation density ρ0 is a robust prediction of relativistic mean-field (RMF) theory [4, 5]. While relativistic mean-field models including hyper-ons are able to describe two solar mass stars [6–10], mi-croscopic approaches based on many-body descriptions do not describe massive stars [11]. If hyperonic degrees of freedom are included, maximum masses of 1.5 M⊙ or below have been obtained [12], see [13] for a review. The inclusion of three-body forces may solve this tension [14– 16]. The recent calculations carried out with a chiral hyperon-nucleon interaction tuned to the pΛ femtoscopic data from the ALICE Collaboration predicted a maxi-mum mass below 1.4 M⊙, though hyperonic three-body forces were not included [17].

The so-called “hyperon puzzle” refers to the tension between the softening of the EOS caused by hyper-ons, which tends to reduce the maximum NS mass be-low 2 M⊙, and the observation of massive pulsars such as PSR J0740+6620 (M ≈ 2.07 M⊙) [18] and PSR J0348+0432 ( M ≈ 2.01 M⊙) [19]. The inclusion of re-pulsive hyperon-hyperon interactions mediated by the hidden-strangeness ϕ meson has been shown to stiffen the EOS sufficiently to accommodate massive NSs even with hyperons present [7, 20], within a RMF description. In this work, we perform a systematic Bayesian com-parison of five RMF model families — each in both nu-cleonic and hyperonic versions — constrained simulta-neously by nuclear saturation properties, χEFT predic-tions for pure neutron matter, pQCD at high density, NICER mass-radius measurements, and GW170817 tidal deformability data. Information from hypernuclei [21] is used to constrain the hyperon-meson couplings. In par-ticular, the couplings determined by reproducing binding energies of hypernuclei within RMF models are taken as reference [22–24]. The possibility of using neutron stars to extract infor-mation on the hyperon content has been considered in references [25, 26]. However, the authors concluded that the present uncertainties on the mass and radius of ob-served neutron stars are still too large and do not allow for drawing any conclusions on the hyperon content. In [27], Bayesian inference was performed within an RMF model with density dependent couplings, to de-termine under which conditions two solar mass neutron stars would contain hyperons. While it was shown that

III. BAYESIAN INFERENCE FRAMEWORK 

The posterior is sampled using PyMultiNest [30] with 4500 live points. The total log-likelihood combines the following constraints: 

a. Nuclear matter properties (NMP). Gaussian con-straints at saturation — binding energy e0 = −16 .0 ±

0.2 MeV, incompressibility K0 = 230 ± 40 MeV, symme-try energy Jsym = 32 .5 ± 2.5 MeV. Computed from the 

nucleonic EOS. 

b. χEFT constraints. Super-Gaussian constraints on pure neutron matter energy density at sub-saturation densities, with 5% enlargement [31]. Computed from the 

nucleonic EOS. 

c. NICER mass-radius. KDE likelihoods from posterior samples of PSR J0030+0451 [32], PSR J0437 −4715 [33], and PSR J0740+6620 [34]. Evaluated on the hyperonic EOS for hyperonic models. 

d. GW170817. Tidal deformability likelihood from the binary NS merger [35, 36]. 

e. pQCD. Constraint at ρ ≈ 8.5 ρ0 using the Komoltsev-Kurkela framework [37]. The key design choice is that NMP and χEFT use the nucleonic code (hyperons do not change saturation or PNM properties), while all astrophysical constraints use the hyperonic EOS.

F. Nuclear matter properties 

Tables V summarises the posterior distributions. The NMP values are nearly identical between nucleonic and hyperonic versions, except for K0 and Ksym . The in-compressibility generally takes a larger value for the hy-peronic EOS (except for DDME), and the difference can be as large as ∼ 40 MeV. This reflects the fact that, in order to describe two solar mass stars, the EOS in-cluding hyperons has to be stiffer to compensate for the softening when hyperons set in. Concerning Ksym , it sys-tematically shows a less negative value for the hyperonic stars, reaching values close to zero or even positive at 90% CI. Since no conditions have been imposed that re-ally constrain composition, such as the proton fraction, the values Ksym takes are probably just contributing to the stiffening of the EOS, in addition to K0.
