Abstract 

We present a spectral analysis of the NuSTAR and NICER observations of the accreting millisecond X-ray pulsar IGR J17062-6143, performed in 2022. The source remained in the hard spectral state during the observations, with a luminosity of about 0.2-1.3 percent of the Eddington luminosity. The continuum emission of the NuSTAR spectrum is entirely domi-nated by a power-law component or by Comptonized emission of disk photons by a plasma with a high electron temperature ( ≳ 100 keV). The NuSTAR spectrum also reveals clear evidence of disk reflection, a broad Fe K line around 6-8 keV, and a Compton hump peaking at 20 keV, irrespective of the choice of the continuum models. Our spectral studies suggest a disk extending close to the neutron star surface ( ∼7-17 Rg) at low inclination angles ( ∼20-40 degrees), as revealed by a couple of self-consistent relativistic reflection models, relxill and 

relxillCP . In addition, we detected type-II bursts for the first time in the NICER observa-tion of this source. Light curve profiles of type-II bursts exhibit different patterns, mostly associated with the so-called mode-0 and mode-1 type-II bursts. The energy spectra of the persistent (pre-burst) and burst emission are well described by an absorbed Comptonization component, scattering diskbb - and blackbody-distributed photons, respectively, by a corona with a temperature of 1-3 keV. Although the origin of the type-II burst is not very clear, it has been substantially linked to magnetospheric gating of the accretion flow. 

Keywords: accretion - Neutron star: X-ray Binary – Neutron Star Low Mass X-ray binary – Accreting Millisecond X-ray Pulsars – disk reflection – disk truncation: spectral analysis – individual IGR J17062-6143 – accretion-powered bursts: type-II burst 

## 1 Introduction 

Neutron Star Low mass X-ray binaries (NS LMXBs) consist of a neutron star (NS) that accretes gas from a companion having less mass than the compact primary (Shapiro and Teukolsky,

1983; Tanaka, 1997; Bahramian and Degenaar, 2022). The accreted matter transferred from the donor generally possesses angular momentum and does not fall directly on the compact object (Lynden-Bell and Pringle, 1974). In a binary system, mass transfer occurs when the donor star fills its Roche lobe, and matter flows through the inner Lagrangian point ( L1) (Roche, 1849; Kopal, 1989). Due to conservation of angular momentum at L1, the accreted matter forms an accretion disk around the compact object (Anzer and Boerner, 1980; Frank et al., 2002). The infalling matter in the accretion disk exerts a ram pressure as a consequence of its inward motion through the disk plasma (Gunn and Gott III, 1972). Neutron stars typically possess magnetic field B ∼ 10 8 − 10 9 G (Thompson and Duncan, 1993). The accretion flow towards the neutron star gets truncated at the Alfv´ en radius ( RA), where the inward ram pressure of the accreting matter is comparable to the magnetic pressure (Ghosh et al., 1977). Inside the Alfv´ enradius, the magnetic field of the neutron star determines the flow of matter. The magnetic field channels the matter along the magnetic field lines towards the magnetic poles of the neutron star. The accreted matter, while falling inward to the magnetic poles of the NS, gets heated up in the due process of acceleration and radiates in X-rays. Accreting millisecond X-ray pulsars (AMXPs) are NS LMXBs where the NS spins up rapidly to high frequency ( ν ≥ 100 Hz) and the accreted gas gets channeled out of the accretion disk by weak magnetic fields (B ∼10 8−9 G) onto the magnetic poles of the neutron star giving rise to the X-ray pulsations at the spin frequency (Patruno and Watts, 2021). The extent to which the inner accretion disk can penetrate toward the neutron star is gov-erned by the magnetic field of the neutron star (Lamb et al., 1973). The accretion disk is trun-cated near magnetospheric radius ( Rm), which is a fraction of the Alfv´ en radius, Rm = ξR A,where ξ(∼ 0.5 − 1) depends on the geometry and disk-magnetosphere interaction (Ghosh and Lamb, 1979). At the magnetospheric radius, the ram pressure of the accreting matter becomes comparable to the magnetic pressure of the NS magnetosphere. The co-rotation radius ( Rco ) is defined as the distance from the neutron star where the angular velocity of matter in a Keplerian disk is equal to the spin angular frequency of the neutron star. Inside the magnetosphere, the accretion flow departs from Keplerian motion and becomes increasingly governed by the mag-netic field (M¨ onkk¨ onen et al., 2022b). The accreted matter is funneled along the magnetic field lines directed towards the magnetic poles of the neutron star (M¨ onkk¨ onen et al., 2022a). Upon impacting the neutron-star surface, the gravitational potential energy of the accreted matter is released as X-ray radiation (Zhu, 2025). When the magnetosphere lies close to the co-rotation radius, disk-magnetosphere interaction becomes unstable, accreted matter may accumulate near the magnetospheric boundary and be episodically released onto the neutron star surface, result-ing in short-lived bursts of X-ray radiation (Salvo et al., 2024). These bursts can be categorized into two types based on their energy source: type-I and type-II. Generally, type-I X-ray bursts arise from unstable thermonuclear ignition of accreted matter accumulated on the neutron star surface (Belian et al., 1976; Lewin et al., 1993). Type-II bursts are attributed to instabilities in the accretion flow rather than thermonuclear burning on the neutron star surface (Bagnoli et al., 2015). The recurrence time between type-II bursts ranges from a few minutes to several hours. During type-I bursts, the temperature correspond-ing to the blackbody emission rises due to unstable thermonuclear burning. After the burst the heated surface layer of the neutron star undergoes radiative cooling, which is shown as the decay phase (Jaisawal et al., 2024). Unlike type-I bursts that show a cooling-tail during decay phase, type-II bursts do not show a pronounced cooling phase and are characterized by nearly constant blackbody temperature throughout the temporal evolution of the burst (Hirotani et al., 1990). 2The source IGR J17062-6143 (hereafter referred to as J17062) was discovered during the 2006 observation of INTEGRAL (Churazov et al., 2007). The 163.65 Hz pulsations detected from the 

RXTE instrument established the source as an AMXP (Strohmayer and Keek, 2017). An upper limit on the magnetic field of B ≲3.5 ×10 8 G was estimated by Strohmayer and Keek (2017). van den Eijnden et al. (2018) estimated a lower limit on the magnetic field of B ≳ 4.8 × 10 6 Gfor low inclination angle. They also reported an observed flux of ∼0.98 × 10 −10 ergs cm −2 s−1.Strohmayer et al. (2018) estimated an inclination angle within 19 ◦ and 27.5 ◦ corresponding to a neutron star of mass 1.4M ⊙ and 2.0M ⊙, respectively. From reflection studies, a broad iron emission line has been confirmed in previous observations of J17062, showing reflection of hard X-rays from the inner accretion disk (Keek et al., 2017; van den Eijnden et al., 2016). Previous studies by Strohmayer and Keek (2017) showed a highly ionized disk with a low accreting flux. It is a persistent source with long-term mass accretion rate ˙M ≃ 2.5 × 10 −11 M⊙ yr −1 (Keek et al., 2017; Strohmayer et al., 2018). Earlier, type-I X-ray bursts have been observed from this source (Degenaar et al., 2013; Keek et al., 2017). For instance, the burst fluences during the observed bursts in 2012 outbursts by Degenaar et al. (2013) were in the range (1 .60 −1.82) ×10 −5 erg cm −2.The blackbody flux was estimated as ∼4.8 ×10 −8 erg cm −2 s−1. Keek et al. ∼6.0 ×10 −8 erg cm −2 s−1. Equating with the Eddington luminosity, the distance to the source was estimated as d = 7 .3 ± 0.5 kpc. In addition, the spectral analysis suggested the reflection region moved significantly closer to the neutron star during the burst, with inner disk radius decreasing from Rin ∼ 200 Rg to ∼ 14 Rg. The inward migration of the disk suggests a strong influence of the burst emission on the accretion flow, leading to a modification of the inner accretion disk geometry. 

NuSTAR ′s unprecedented sensitivity and broad energy bandwidth, free of pileup distortions, allow us to clearly map the reflection features in various neutron star X-ray binaries. The source J17062 was effectively observed by NuSTAR three times between 2015 and 2022. Previous NuS-TAR observations performed between 2015 and 2016 were analyzed by different authors, and broad-band spectral analysis revealed reflection features from the highly ionized disk (Degenaar et al., 2017; Keek et al., 2017). From the reflection spectroscopy, Degenaar et al. (2017) inferred a truncated disk very far from the neutron star surface at ≳100 Rg. The inferred inner radius is significantly larger than typically observed in accreting neutron stars. Keek et al. (2017) also performed spectral analysis of the 2015 NuSTAR observation and suggested a larger inner disk radius of ∼ 210 Rg from the spectral fits. Later, van den Eijnden et al. (2018) analyzed the 2016 NuSTAR observation along with the 2015 observation. They found a strongly trun-cated accretion disk at ∼ 77 Rg, assuming a high inclination. They also reported that the data quality of the 2016 NuSTAR observation was not sufficient to constrain the full broad-band relativistic reflection spectrum. However, in all such studies, some important parameters were not well constrained by the different reflection models. For example, Degenaar et al. (2017) could not constrain the inclination angle using the reflection model. Moreover, the inferred inner radius was barely constrained. Although Rin exceeded 100 Rg, the RISCO could not be excluded. van den Eijnden et al. (2018) also noted that a low inclination and a disc extending to the neutron star cannot be ruled out. Keeping those in mind, we performed spectral analysis of the most recent NuSTAR obser-vation to constrain various parameters precisely. ## 2 Observation and Data reduction 

Nuclear Spectroscopic Telescopic Array (NuSTAR ), launched by NASA on 2012 June 13, is the first focusing hard X-ray telescope operating in the high energy (3 − 79 keV) range (Harrison et al., 2013). Neutron Star Interior Composition Explorer (NICER ) mission is dedicated towards the study of thermal and non-thermal emission from neutron stars in the 0.2-12 keV (soft) X-ray band (Gendreau et al., 2016). The analyses presented in this work are based on observations of J17062 conducted by NuSTAR and NICER as tabulated in Table 1. The NuSTAR data were processed using the data analysis software NuSTARDAS v0.4.12 ,within HEASOFT v6.36 employing the latest calibration database ( CALDB v20260421 ). The task 

nupipeline v0.4.12 was used to generate the calibrated and screened event files. For all the observations a circular extraction region of 120 ′′ radius was used to study the persistent and burst spectrum. From the same chip, the background was selected as a region of radius 120 ′′ far away from the source. HEASOFT provides FTOOLS to deduce the F IT S files needed for analysing the observed data. The FTOOL nuproducts was used to extract the spectra and light curves from FPMA and FPMB detectors. The NICER standard calibration, screening, and filtering of the data is done using the nicerl2 v1.41 pipeline (Remillard et al., 2022). The lightcurve were produced using nicerl3-lc task. The screened files obtained from nicerl2 pipeline are further utilised to extract the required files for spectral analysis using nicerl3-spect task. For studying the bursts, good time interval (GTI) files were created separately for the per-sistent emission and the observed type-II X-ray burst(s) by using the maketime task on the mkf 

file located in auxil directory of the NICER observation. During burst analysis, data above 9 keV were excluded due to background dominance. The details of the observation IDs included in this paper are provided in Table 1. Hereafter, the NuSTAR observation ID 30801032002, and 

NICER observation IDs 5034100109, 5531010105, and 5531010215 are referred to as Obs 1, 2, 43, and 4, respectively.

The long-term MAXI /GSC light curve of the source J17062 is shown in the upper left panel of Figure 1, indicating the NuSTAR FPMA/B observation considered for the present analysis (Matsuoka et al., 2009). The complete NuSTAR FPMA/FPMB and NICER /XRT light curve corresponding to Obs 1, 2 and 3 are shown in the upper right , lower left and lower right panel of Fig. 1. NuSTAR observation (Obs 1) presents an overall persistent emission having a count rate within ∼ 1.5-2.5 counts s −1. For NICER observation (Obs 2), the count rate during persistent emission varies within ∼4-7 counts s −1. We observe a region during the initial exposure of Obs 2, wherein the count rate reaches 22 counts s −1 within ∼200 s. The peak emission stalls for 

∼150s duration before falling sharply back to 5 counts s −1 within 35s. Although there is a ∼5times increase in the net count rate, the event does not adhere to a burst profile. We are now making a difference with burst regions. Essentially, we refer to such intervals where the emission rises to a peak value within a certain duration and then decays to its prior level as a flaring region. For the Obs 2, 3, and 4, the net count rates corresponding to the persistent emission and peak regions – depending on the presence of flaring or bursting intervals within the observations – are listed in Table 1. For Obs 3 and 4, the peak emissions correspond to the bursts with the maximum count rate. The net count rate for the persistent emission for Obs 3 varies within 

∼10-30 counts s −1. For Obs 4, the persistent emission remains within ∼10-40 counts s −1. Apart from light curve studies, we have not considered Obs 2 and 4 further. We mainly focus on Obs 3. From the light curve of the Obs 3, as shown in the lower right panel of Fig. 1, five prominent flaring regions are observed. These flares show burst-like features and are identified as five dis-tinct burst events, as indicated by small red arrows in the light curve. Out of the five observed bursts, we have considered the first three ( viz. B1, B2, and B3), for which the full rise and de-cay regions were captured during the seventh , eighth , and ninth NICER cycle of the particular exposure. The light curves corresponding to the rise, peak and decay of the bursts are plotted, with a horizontal line indicating the persistent emission level, and are shown in the upper panels of Fig. 2. The peak count rate during B1, B2 and B3 are ∼113, ∼843 and ∼138 counts s −1,respectively. We observe that the burst profiles do not clearly indicate the characteristics of fast rise and exponential decay (FRED), which are commonly observed in type-I bursts. We therefore attributed the observed bursts as type-II bursts. The light curve profiles of the type-II bursts differ from those of type-I bursts in a number of features. Type-II bursts exhibit a variety of morphological modes that may suddenly switch between them. Type-II bursts are the most energetic, longest, and least frequent, commonly associated with a hard spectral state when the persistent luminosity is below 10 percent of the Eddington limit. In our case, we have observed type-II bursts with different morphologies/patterns. The first burst of NICER Obs 3 (B1) is about ∼350 seconds long and has a flat peak lasting at least 

∼100 seconds. The burst observed in NICER Obs 4 has similar characteristics: a long duration (around ∼250 seconds), a high intensity, and a long plateau. These features are related to the so-called mode-0 type-II bursts (Bagnoli et al., 2015; Wang and Ji, 2026). However, the charac-teristics of the second (B2) and third (B3) bursts observed from NICER Obs 3 are completely different. They are associated with a series of short, rapidly recurring bursts. Also, they exhibit multiple peaks in their decay phase. This bursting pattern is called mode-1. Their rising and peak phases were occulted, and hence were not included for burst analysis. There is a significant time lapse during the rising phase of each of the bursts B1, B2 and B3. In our analysis, we considered the burst initiation level as the time where the count rate exceeds 50% of the persistent net count rate. The burst rise time is considered as the time taken to reach 90% of the peak burst count rate (Galloway et al., 2008). Time duration for peak burst is the duration for which the emission remains above 90% of the peak level. Time taken for the emission to go below the burst initiation level is considered the decay time. For B2 and B3, another lower maximum is observed after the peak burst. The details for the bursts of Obs 3 are listed in Table 2. 

## 4 Spectral Analysis 

4.1 NuSTAR 

4.1.1 Persistent emission 

The spectral analysis for the persistent and burst emission was carried out using XSPEC v 12.15.1 (Arnaud, 1996). To account for the interstellar absorption along the line of sight, we have used 8the TBabs model. Initially, we tried to fit the 3-30 keV NuSTAR FPMA and FPMB spectra simultaneously with an absorbed power-law model. It provided a good description of the con-tinuum emission with χ2/dof = 1177/542 and a power-law photon index, Γ, of around ∼2.5. The best-fit spectral plot is shown in the left panel of Figure 3, along with the fit residuals. We then introduced the physically motivated model nthcomp to fit the continuum emission instead of a power-law (Zdziarski et al., 1996; ˙Zycki et al., 1999). It is a thermal Comptonization model, which involves a seed photon source as a blackbody or a disk-like multi-color blackbody. The seed photons are up-scattered by hot electrons, resulting in the output spectrum. We considered a geometry in which the seed photons are arising from the disk. It provided χ2/dof =1081/540 (∆ χ2 =-96 for 2 dof ) with photon index (Γ) and disk temperature ( kT in ) of around ∼2.54 and 

∼0.75 keV, respectively. However, the fit revealed a high electron temperature ( kT e) of around 

∼550 keV. The corresponding spectral plot for the is shown in the middle panel of Figure 3. We finally replaced the nthcomp with its updated version, thcomp convolved with diskbb .

Thcomp describes the continuum Comptonization emission much better than a cutoff power law model, and it aligns more closely with the actual Monte Carlo spectra from Comptonization than nthcomp does. The thcomp model estimates the Comptonization distribution of seed pho-tons from the disk, that are upscattered by the corona, as well as, the neutron star surface simultaneously (Zdziarski et al., 1996; Nied´ zwiecki et al., 2019; Zdziarski et al., 2020). It further improved the fit with χ2/dof =860/539 ∼1.60 (∆ χ2=-221 for 1 dof ). The best-fit spectral plot for the model const*TBabs*(thcomp*diskbb) is shown in the right panel of Figure 3. For the two continuum models, the electron temperature reached the upper hard limit provided by the models, namely 1000 keV for nthcomp and 1500 keV for thcomp*diskbb , respectively. The parameters for thcomp*diskbb components, photon index (Γ τ ), multicolor disk temperature (kT in ), electron temperature ( kT e) and covering fraction ( fcov ) are estimated around ∼ 1.90, 1.41 keV, ≳ 100 keV and ∼ 0.34, respectively. Our continuum modeling showed no evidence for an observable high-energy cutoff ( Ecut ) in the NuSTAR bandpass. Since the high-energy cutoff is related to the electron temperature of the corona, the electrons in the plasma must be very hot ( kT e ≳100 keV), as reflected in the fits. A high Ecut value is also evident from an earlier NuSTAR observation in 2015 (Degenaar et al., 2017; Strohmayer and Keek, 2017). Moreover, all choices of the continuum models exhibit strong residuals around 5-8 keV and 20-30 keV, signatures of the broad Fe emission line and Compton hump, respectively. It shows the robustness of the detection of the reflection features from the NuSTAR spectrum. Detection of these reflection features in the spectrum (see Fig. 3) motivates further use of self-consistent reflection models to describe them. 

4.1.2 Reflection spectroscopy 

The hard X-ray emission produced by Comptonization in the corona irradiates the accretion disk and is reprocessed, giving rise to a reflection spectrum. The reflected Comptonized emis-sion provides a probe into the physical properties and geometry of the accretion disk. To model the reflection spectrum, we used the relativistic reflection model relxill (Dauser, T. et al., 2016). It describes the reflection of a power-law continuum, originating from the corona, from the accretion disk. The model parameters corresponding to the relxill component are inclina-tion angle of the source to the line of sight ( i), inner disk radius ( Rin ), outer disk radius ( Rout ), breaking radius ( Rbr ), emissivity index q1 between Rin and Rout and q2 between Rout and Rbr ,spin parameter ( a∗), photon index (Γ), ionization of the accretion disk (log ξ), iron abundance (AFe ), high energy cut-off of the observed spectrum ( Ecut ), reflection fraction, redshift ( z) and 9normalization. We also used the relxillCP model, which is a self-consistent reflection model. It provides a physically motivated description of the disk-reflected Comptonized coronal-emission. Except Ecut , all other parameters of relxill , along with the two parameters, viz. , density of the accretion disk (log N )and electron temperature ( kT e), are estimated by the relxillCP model. We used the two models, Model 1a – const*TBabs*relxill , and Model 1b –

const*TBabs*relxillCP ), to fit the persistent and reflection emission and thereby ascer-taining the inner radius of the accretion disk ( Rin ) and the angle of inclination of the source (i). During the analysis, the neutral hydrogen column depth density ( NH) is kept constant at 

∼ 0.1 × 10 22 cm −2 (van der Klis et al., 1985; Dickey and Lockman, 1990). The breaking radius and outer disk radius were frozen at 1000 Rg. The emissivity indices ( q1 and q2) were frozen at 3, corresponding to the standard value of a centrally radiating source. The dimensionless spin parameter ( a∗) was calculated numerically utilizing the maximum spin parameter for neutron stars in LMXBs ( jmax ∼ 0.7; Lo and Lin 2011) and the pulsation frequency ( f ) of J17062 (f = 163 .65 Hz). The maximum breakup frequency ( fk) is considered within 1000-1500 Hz. We considered the breakup frequency ∼1400 Hz. For slowly rotating stars ( a∗ < 0.3), the spin parameter ( a∗) scales as a∗ = jmax ffk . The value obtained is a∗ ≃ 0.1, which was frozen during the spectral fitting. The redshift was fixed at z = 0, as the emission and absorption processes were modeled in the rest frame of the source. For the Model 1a and Model 1b, we obtained best-fit statistics χ2/dof as 516 /536 ∼ 0.96 and 502 /535 ∼ 0.94, respectively. The best-fit values corresponding to models 1a and 1b are listed in Table 3. The constant parameter that ensures calibration among FPMA and FPMB resulted in 0 .99 ± 0.01 for both models. The 1σ upper limit of the inner disk radius and inclination angle were estimated at 2 .98 RISCO 

and 1 .14 RISCO ; and ∼ 41 ◦ and ∼ 21 ◦, respectively, for relxill and relillCP models. The photon index for the two models was quite similar as Γ ∼ 2.38 and ∼2.71 for Model 1a and 1b, respectively. We obtained the reflection fraction as ∼ 1.11 for Model 1a and ∼ 0.19 for Model 1b. Disk ionization (log ξ) and iron abundance ( AFe ) parameters were estimated for the 

relxill and relxillCP models at ∼ 3.83 and ∼ 3.40; and, ∼ 1.87 and ∼ 4.96, respectively. These values imply a highly ionized disk with high iron abundance exceeding the solar value, consistent with the previous studies. As, we obtained high electron temperature corresponding to the continuum models, in a similar manner, for Model 1a ( relxill ) the cutoff energy ( Ecut )provided a minimum value ∼830keV, and for Model 1b ( relxillCP ), electron temperature (kT e) was associated with large uncertainties. The electron density parameter (log N ) of the 

relxillCP component was valued at ∼ 19 .1, indicating a highly dense accretion disk. The spectral plots corresponding to the best-fit models 1a and 1b are shown in Figure 4. The steppar command was used to assess the robustness of the best-fit parameters obtained from Model 1a and 1b. We then tried an alternative model for the type-II X-ray burst: an absorbed Comptonization model, previously used by Bagnoli et al. (2015) and Wang and Ji (2026). We applied bbodyrad*thcomp to fit the burst spectra, considering that the seed photons from the neutron star surface/boundary layer are up-scattered in a Comptonizing corona. Therefore, we had to add a power-law model to fit the excess at higher energies ( >5 keV) and a Gaussian to fit the excess around 2.3 keV. The best-fit parameter values for the power-law and Gaussian models are consistent with persistent emission. We set the power-law photon index (Γ) to −3.0 in all cases. This model combination yielded a reasonable fit, with χ2/dof of 103 /96, 152 /105, and 86 /81 for bursts 1, 2, and 3, respectively. All the best-fit parameter values are listed in the Table 4. Although the excess near 2 .2 keV may be an instrumental artifact rather than a real emission line, the origin of the hard power-law component with a negative slope remains unclear. It may originate from Comptonization in a hybrid thermal/non-thermal electron corona associated with the compact object (see, e.g., Farinelli et al. 2005; Poutanen and Coppi 1998). However, a notable result from the fit of the persistent and time-averaged burst emission in the NICER observation was the seed photon temperature during the persistent emission, characterized by a multi-temperature blackbody ( diskbb ) of ∼0.32-0.38 keV, which is slightly higher than the seed photon temperature of ∼0.26 keV, characterized by a single-temperature blackbody ( bbodyrad ), during the burst emission, usually interpreted as the emission from the neutron star surface or boundary layer. This discrepancy suggests that the seed photon during the persistent phase might be from the hotter part of the accretion flow/boundary layer, fed by mass accretion. At the same time, the Comptonized emission during the burst may be associated with the comparatively cooler part of the accretion flow. 

4.3 Time-resolved analysis of the burst spectra 

We performed the time-resolved spectral analysis of one of the most intense and energetic bursts (B2, peak count ∼850 counts/s) observed in the NICER Obs 3 listed in Table 1. We intended to track the evolution of the Comptonized emission from the neutron star surface 15 during the burst. Because the light curve of the burst shows a slow rise and fall after rapid recurring bursts (see Figure 2), we excluded the first 180 seconds and the last 20 seconds from our analysis. We divided the intermediate time interval into 19 segments, each 10 seconds long. We extracted spectra for each interval and attempted to fit them with the bbodyrad*thcomp 

model, as applied to the time-averaged burst spectrum earlier. For individual sections, we also applied powerlaw and Gaussian models. Initially, we let the model parameters of the powerlaw 

and the Gaussian model be free during the fitting. However, we found that the parameter values are consistent with the persistent values and with those obtained from spectral analysis of the time-averaged burst analysis. The same methodology was applied to the thcomp model parameters. We let the power-law photon index (Γ τ ), the electron temperature ( kT e), and the covering fraction ( fcov ) free during the fitting. We found that the power-law photon index (Γ τ )is pegged at its lower value ∼1 and that the covering fraction ( fcov ) changes little, remaining within ∼0.3-0.6. Hence, we fixed Γ τ and fcov , along with other parameters, to explore variations in the temperature of the seed photons ( kT bb ) and the electron temperature of the hot plasma (kT e), as well as the normalization of the Comptonized emission during the burst. The spectral fitting resulted in the seed photon temperature kT bb ∼ 0.15 − 0.26 keV and the electron temperature of the hot plasma kT e ∼ 1 − 3 keV. The resulting parameter variations are shown in the Figure 7. We also note that the parameter variations are very similar to those measured after keeping the other Comptonized emission-related parameters free during the fitting. 

## 5 Discussion 

We performed a spectral analysis of two observations of the accreting millisecond pulsar J17062 obtained with NICER and NuSTAR on 2022 April 25 and September 19, respectively. The unabsorbed 0 .1 − 100 keV bolometric fluxes during the NuSTAR (Obs 1) and NICER (Obs 3) observations are 1.10 ×10 −10 and 7.94 ×10 −10 ergs cm −2 s−1, respectively. The observed luminosities correspond to ∼0.2% and 1.33% of LEdd , respectively (where the Eddington luminosity is given as LEdd = 3 .8 × 10 38 ergs s −1, considering the neutron star of mass M = 1.4M ⊙ and radius R=10 km). The estimated values of the fluxes, luminosities, and mass accretion rates (listed in Table 5) during Obs 1 and 3 indicate that the source remained in the so-called hard spectral state during these observations. The continuum emission of the NuSTAR 

spectrum in the energy band 3 − 30 keV is entirely dominated by a power-law component or by Comptonized emission of disk photons of temperature ∼ 0.75 − 1.40 keV by a plasma with a high electron temperature ( kT e ≳ 100 keV) and a small covering fraction, fcov ∼ 0.34. In addition to continuum emission, prominent reflection features were detected, including a broad Fe K emission line around 5 − 8 keV and a Compton hump around 15 − 30 keV. The presence of such features strongly suggests a reflection of hard coronal photons from the accretion disk. We used two self-consistent relativistic reflection models (1a and 1b), relxill 

and relxillCP , respectively, to investigate the parameters associated with the disk. The inner radius ( Rin ) and inclination angle ( i) of the accretion disk are primary to ascertain the truncation of the disk. Table 3 shows that the best-fit values of both model parameters obtained by relxill and relxillCP are well constrained within 1 σ uncertainty. Our reflection fit with

The result was consistent with the different choice of the inclination angle (as it was not well constrained by the data). However, they also noted that Rin was consistent with a location at the ISCO at 3 σ confidence for 

i = 65 ◦. From the reflection studies, Strohmayer and Keek (2017) suggested that the inner disk is truncated at ∼ 100 Rg before the burst, but may move closer to the star during the burst. Although van den Eijnden et al. (2018) detected a truncated disc at around 77 Rg,assuming a high inclination angle, they could not rule out the possibility of a disk extending to the neutron star at low inclination. Our spectral studies suggested a disk extending close to the neutron star surface at low inclination, as revealed by a couple of the self-consistent reflection models. Therefore, the results are consistent with the earlier predictions of different authors based on reflection studies. However, in the low accretion rates, it is difficult to definitively distinguish whether the NS’s magnetic field truncates the disk, or the formation of a hot inner flow results in a large inner disk radius. This indicates a substantial change in the accretion geometry of the system over time. Nevertheless, the greater uncertainties associated with the earlier results, along with the present inference of a disk extending close to the NS rather than exhibiting a large truncation from two reflection models, may in-dicate that the previously reported disk estimates were overestimated owing to systematic biases. The log ξ parameter, which signifies the logarithm of ionization of the accretion disk, is estimated at ∼3.83 and ∼3.40 for the reflection models relxill and relxilCP , respectively. The iron abundance ( AFe ) of the accretion disk was found to be ∼1.2-5 times the solar value, indicating a high iron abundance in the disc. During the persistent spectral fitting, the power-law component converged to a negative photon index (Γ ∼-3.0), indicating an unphysical continuum that rises with energy. This behavior is likely driven by high-energy residual features 18 that are not adequately described by the adopted spectral model. As the power law component tries to fit the high energy residuals, it adopts an unphysical slope. Therefore, to minimize the influence of the high-energy excess and obtain stable spectral fits, we restricted our analy-sis to the 0 .7−9 keV energy range and fixed the photon index Γ at -3.0 during the burst analysis. Previously, long and energetic type-I thermonuclear X-ray bursts have been detected by 

Swif t /XRT and M AXI /GSC, which decay as straight power laws, described by a single linear function with slopes of −0.32 to −1.15 (Keek et al., 2017). For the first time, five type-II bursts were detected during Obs 3 (see Fig. lower panel of Fig. 1). To date, only two sources exhibit type-II bursts: one is a bursting pulsar (BP), GRO J1744-28 (Kouveliotou et al., 1996), and the other is a rapid burster (RB), MXB 1730-335 (Lewin et al., 1976). Considering our detection of type-II bursts from the accreting pulsar J17062, it becomes the third object from which type-II bursts have been detected. Previously, long type-I X-ray bursts have been observed from the source Degenaar et al. (2013); Keek et al. (2017). The source becomes the rarest one, probably the second pulsar, which exhibits both types of bursts. To understand the origin and behavior of type II X-ray bursts, a series of models have been proposed, focusing on the sudden release of the accretion power in the disk. These include disk–magnetosphere interactions (Jiang et al., 2014), trapped disks or the magnetic barrier (D’Angelo and Spruit, 2012), instabilities in the accretion flow, and thermal or viscous instabilities in the accretion disk (Lightman and Eardley, 1974; Shakura and Sunyaev, 1976). We have analysed the time-averaged burst spectrum corresponding to the three bursts B1, B2 and B3 using the model TBabs*(thcomp*bbodyrad+power law+Gaussian) in the energy range 0 .7 − 9 keV. From time-averaged spectrum, the blackbody temperature of the neutron star surface or boundary layer during B1, B2, and B3 is estimated as ∼0.24, ∼0.22, and ∼0.16 keV, respectively. Time-resolved spectral analysis (Figure 7) revealed that the electron temperature ( kT e) of the corona and the seed photon temperature ( kT bb ) showed no significant variability during the burst. kT e and kT bb , in general, vary between 1 − 3 keV and 0 .15 − 0.23 keV, respectively. For most segments, the reduced χ2 remained very close to unity or lower. However, the normalization of the Comptonized emission showed notable variability, resembling the intensity variation during the burst. This result signifies that the burst emission may not originate in the neutron surface and possibly involves inner accretion flow, an extended emission or reprocessing region, or a boundary layer. From the time-resolved burst spectroscopy, we did not observe any significant cooling effect for the corona during the burst. The corona temperature remained roughly ∼2 keV, consistent with other ASCA 

(Mahasena et al., 2003) and RXTE (Bagnoli et al., 2015) observations. As our NuSTAR 

observation suggests a Comptonizing coronal region with an electron temperature ≳ 100 keV, these results support the idea of an additional, temporary coronal region with a temperature around 2 keV. Although the exact mechanism by which such a region forms is not clear, it may be linked with the magnetic reconnection on the disk (Chen et al., 2021). The inward motion of the accreting material is governed by the competition between the ram pressure of the accretion flow and the magnetic pressure of the neutron-star magnetosphere. For the bursts B1, B2 and B3, we have obtained α within 1-11. Such low values are inconsistent with thermonuclear (type-I) bursts, for which the typical values lie within 40 − 100. On the contrary, type-II bursts are essentially characterized by a low α value (below 20) that signifies the bursts being powered by episodic accretion (Wang and Ji, 2026). The observed α

values strongly support an accretion-driven origin of the bursts. Moreover, type-II bursts have a long waiting time between bursts, which is needed for the accumulation of fuel. As demonstrated by Wang and Ji (2026), type-II bursts are often characterized by a positive correlation between burst fluence and the waiting time preceding the burst, reflecting the accumulation of accreting material in a reservoir prior to its sudden release. We consider the waiting time between the three bursts B1-B2, B2-B3, and B4-B5 as 5559.7s, 5583s, and 5740s, respectively, from the full light curve of the NICER Obs 3. By correlating these waiting times with the burst fluences of B2, B3, and B5 from Table 6, we obtained the Pearson correlation coefficient (see Appendix B). As there are three bursts, we get n = 3 and considering the waiting time between bursts and burst fluences for i = 1 , 2, 3as variables xi and yi, provides the Pearson correlation coefficient as r = 0 .99. The p-value derived from the Student’s t-statistic is 0 .04, indicating a positive correlation between waiting time and burst fluence. Although the statistical significance is limited by the small number of bursts, the result favours the observation of accretion-powered type-II bursts. Although the occurrence of type-I bursts has been previously reported from J17062 by De-genaar et al. (2013), our analysis suggests that at least some of the light curve profiles are associated with type-II bursts. The estimations of a low α value, the positive correlation be-21 tween waiting time and burst fluence, and the magnetospheric radius being very close to the co-rotation radius, suggest an accretion-driven origin of the bursts. The system may be accret-ing in a magnetospheric gating or trapped-disk regime, in which matter accumulates near the disk-magnetosphere boundary. As the accumulated matter increases the ram pressure at the inner disk edge, the magnetosphere is pushed inside the co-rotation radius. The accumulated matter rapidly reaches the neutron star’s surface. The accretion rate increases and is short-lived, which results in bursts of X-ray emission. In our case, the disk-magnetosphere interaction may provide an explanation for the observations of type-II-like bursts from J17062. Although van den Eijnden et al. (2018) suggested J17062 was operating near a propeller-dominated regime based on 2015 observations, the smaller inner disk obtained from the 2022 NuSTAR observation suggests that the source is in an active accretion regime. The inner disk extends sufficiently close to the neutron star surface, enabling sustained accretion and X-ray emission. However, in-depth studies on type-II bursts are required to shed more light on this.

as correlation coefficient ( r) which is given by

where, ¯ x and ¯ y refers to the mean values of xi and yi, respectively. The r-value is inter-preted as positive, null and negative correlation for r=+1, 0, and -1, respectively. The corresponding p-value is obtained from Student’s t-statistic (Student, 1908; Peters, 1987). The Student’s t-statistic is given by t = r

q n−21−r2 . And, the two-sided p-value (significance) is then p = 2[1 − Ft(|t|, ν )], where Ft is the cumulative distribution function of the Student’s
