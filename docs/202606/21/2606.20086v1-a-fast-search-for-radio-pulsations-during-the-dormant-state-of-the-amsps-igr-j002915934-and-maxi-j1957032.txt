ABSTRACT 

Accreting millisecond pulsars (AMSPs) and transitional millisecond pulsars (tMSPs) are neutron star low-mass X-ray binaries which can evolve into "recycled" radio millisecond pulsars. In both types of systems, X-ray pulsations have been detected during phases of X-ray activity when matter accretion through a disc is turned on. On the other hand, when accretion stops, and these systems enter the quiescent, low-luminosity X-ray state, only tMSPs become visible as radio pulsars. Despite several attempts, radio pulsations have never been detected in quiescent AMSPs, except for IGR J18245 −2452. In this manuscript, we present the results of two observational campaigns performed on the AMSPs IGR J00291 +5934 and MAXI J1957 +032 with the Five-hundred-meter Aperture Spherical Telescope ( FAST ) in L-band (1-1.5 GHz). Both sources have most likely been observed in quiescence, as suggested by the upper limits on their X-ray and optical flux obtained with Swift and the Las Cumbres Observatory, respectively. We have performed a deep search for coherent periodicities in radio but found no significant candidate signal, either at the known spin frequency of those sources or at other frequencies. Assuming a pulse duty cycle of 10%, we derive upper limits on the pulsed radio flux density of 3.3 μJy and 5.6 μJy for IGR J00291 +5934 and MAXI J1957 +032, respectively, which are the most stringent limits so far for any known persistent AMSP.

1. Introduction 

Accreting millisecond pulsars (AMSPs 1) and transitional mil-lisecond pulsars (tMSPs) are classes of low-mass X-ray binaries (LMXBs) that harbour X-ray pulsars spinning at frequencies of about hundreds of Hz (see Campana & Di Salvo 2018; Patruno & Watts 2021; Di Salvo & Sanna 2022; Papitto & de Martino 2022; Di Salvo et al. 2023, for reviews). These systems are tran-sient, as they spend most of their life in a low-luminosity quies-cent X-ray state ( LX ≲ 10 33 erg s −1), where matter accretion is mostly shut-o ff , interrupted by sporadic episodes of X-ray activ-ity. consistently yielded non-detections (Burgay et al. 2003; Iacol-ina et al. 2009, 2010; Patruno et al. 2017; Sanna et al. 2018; Gusinskaia et al. 2020; Li et al. 2026), with the most stringent flux density limits ranging between 2.9–5.7 μJy (in L-band, Peng et al. 2025). The only AMSP that e ff ectively switches on as a ra-dio pulsar in quiescence is IGR J18245 −2452, which belongs to both classes (Papitto et al. 2013). The characteristic swinging be-tween accretion-powered pulsar states and rotation-powered pul-sar states observed in tMSPs can be considered an observational key to confirm the so-called "pulsar recycling scenario" (Alpar et al. 1982). According to this hypothesis, radio millisecond pul-sars (MSPs; see Lorimer 2008; Borghese & Esposito 2023, for reviews) are descendants of accreting NSs in LMXBs binaries, having been spun-up by prolonged mass accretion from a low-mass companion star. Given their importance, considerable e ff orts have been de-voted to identifying new tMSP candidates. On the one hand, these searches have targeted unidentified γ-ray sources - most likely belonging to the "spider" pulsars family (Eichler & Levin-son 1988; Roberts 2013) - that exhibit a sub-luminous “disc” state similar to tMSPs, (e.g. Bogdanov & Halpern 2015; Coti Zelati et al. 2019, 2021, 2024; Jaodand et al. 2021; Manca et al. 2025; Gusinskaia et al. 2025; Kyer et al. 2025). On the other hand, it remains unclear whether at least some AMSPs may switch on as radio pulsars in quiescence and thereby behave as tMSPs as well. Solving this conundrum would have repercus-sions on several aspects of accretion, binary and pulsar physics. Indeed, although both AMSPs and tMSPs can be considered pro-genitors of radio MSPs, it remains unclear whether they con-stitute distinct evolutionary classes or they represent di ff erent phases of a continuous evolutionary sequence. Additionally, de-tecting radio pulsar activation in quiescence would help explain the secular spin-down observed in some AMSPs (Di Salvo & Sanna 2022), and shed light on how the neutron star magne-tosphere and the accretion flow interact at low mass-accretion rates. Expanding the sample of quiescent AMSPs observed in radio, performing regular, high-cadence monitoring programs and /or taking advantage of more sensitive instruments are there-fore crucial ways to address this open question. As the largest single-dish radio telescope in the world, no instrument is bet-ter suited to the latter quest than FAST . Unfortunately, besides Aql X −1, only two of the 26 sources in the AMSPs class (see Di Salvo et al. 2023; Ng et al. 2024, for updated lists), i.e. IGR J00291 +5934 and MAXI J1957 +032, are visible from the 

FAST location. In this manuscript, we report on FAST observa-tions performed on both targets. The manuscript is organized as follows. We present the target sources and their main properties in Sections 1.1–1.2, describe the multi-band data reduction in Section 2, outline the analysis and radio pulsation searches in Section 3, and discuss the results in the context of current litera-ture and future prospects in Section 4. 

> 1.1. IGR J00291+5934

IGR J00291 +5934 holds the record as the fastest-spinning NS among AMSPs with a spin frequency of ∼599 Hz (Markwardt et al. 2004). Since its discovery in 2005 (Shaw et al. 2005), the system displayed three other short outbursts: two in 2008 (Lewis et al. 2010) and one in 2015 (Patruno 2017; De Falco et al. 2017; Sanna et al. 2017b), with a duration of 14, 24 and 25 days, re-spectively. The orbital period is 2.46 hrs, while the distance of the system has been estimated to be 4.2 ±0.5 kpc from the anal-ysis of a photospheric radius expansion type-I X-ray burst (De Falco et al. 2017). Optical observations of the system in qui-escence suggest an irradiated companion star, attributed to the wind of a radio pulsar (D’Avanzo et al. 2007). Furthermore, an episode of intense optical flaring activity (e.g. Baglio et al. 2017) hinted at the presence of an accretion disc even at low X-ray luminosity, somewhat reminiscent of the sub-luminous activity observed in tMSPs (Papitto et al. 2018; Kennedy et al. 2018). However, to our knowledge, no search for radio emission in the system during quiescence has been conducted thus far. 

> 1.2. MAXI J1957+032

MAXI J1957 +032 is an ultra-compact X-ray binary system (Ar-mas Padilla et al. 2023) with an orbital period of only ∼61 mins (Bult et al. 2022). A constraint on the source distance of about 5±2 kpc was placed by comparing the observed optical mag-nitude of the companion star with the one expected for a late-K/early-M dwarf star (Ravi 2017). The behaviour of the source is intriguing, as it shows frequent, i.e. four in about one year (Sugimoto et al. 2015; Tanaka et al. 2016), but short (i.e. ∼days) and faint outbursts, with peak luminosities of about 10 36 erg s −1

or lower (Mata Sánchez et al. 2017; Beri et al. 2019), the most recent one in May 2025 (Illiano et al. 2025; Sanna et al. 2026). Although MAXI J1957 +032 has been known for over a decade, it was identified as a 3.2-ms AMSP only during an outburst in 2022 (Sanna et al. 2022). 

2. Observations & Data Reduction 

> 2.1. FAST

The FAST Telescope (Nan et al. 2011) is currently the largest and most sensitive single-dish radio telescope on Earth, making it the best instrument to probe faint radio emission. Two 50-min observations of IGR J00291 +5934 were performed with FAST 

on 2022 September 22 and October 23. MAXI J1957 +032 was observed once on 2023 October 8 for 20 minutes (see Tables 1-2). All observations were carried out in Tracking mode with the central beam of the L-band 19-beam receiver. Search-mode data were recorded with the ROACH-2 backend over a frequency range of 1 to 1.5 GHz. The 500-MHz band was sampled at a rate of 49.152 μs into 4096 channels, corresponding to a chan-nel width of 0.122 MHz. We scheduled the observations such that the pulsars were as close as possible to zenith at the FAST 

site and near inferior conjunction in their respective orbits based on the extrapolation of the orbital ephemerides of these systems (based on the orbital ephemerides from Sanna et al. 2017b for IGR J00291 +5934 and Sanna et al. 2022 for MAXI J1957 +032). This strategy was adopted to minimize the probability of non-detection caused by eclipses of the radio signal by circumbinary material, which is more likely when the pulsar is near superior conjunction.

3. Data analysis 

Data analysis was performed using the PRESTO 3 (Ransom et al. 2002) pulsar analysis software. The rfifind tool was used to excise radio frequency interference (RFI) from the data. We searched the data for radio emission using several meth-ods, which are described in the sections below. Because the dispersion measure (DM) of each source is unknown, this pa-rameter had to be explored blindly in all searches. Along the line of sight (LoS) toward IGR J00291 +5934, and as-suming a distance of 4.2 kpc, the NE2001 (Cordes & Lazio 2002) and YMW16 (Yao et al. 2017) Galactic electron den-sity models predict DM values of approximately 130 and 165 pc cm −3, respectively, with maximum Galactic DMs of 220 and 260 pc cm −3. For MAXI J1957 +032, at the estimated dis-tance of 5 kpc, the NE2001 and YMW16 models predict DMs of 

∼106 and 73 pc cm −3, and maximum Galactic DMs of 138 and 107 pc cm −3. Given the substantial uncertainties typically asso-ciated with DM predictions from Galactic electron density mod-els, as well as the uncertainties in the source distances (see Sec-tions 1.1–1.2), we searched over trial DM values ranging from 0 to 400 pc cm −3 in each search method—well above the maxi-mum Galactic DM predicted for both systems. To prevent intra-channel dispersion smearing from reducing pulse detectability, we used a DM step of 0.1 pc cm −3, resulting in a total of 4000 DM trials.

> 3.1.2. Acceleration search

Given the several-year gap between the epoch of our FAST ob-servations and the epochs of the reference X-ray timing solu-tions, as well as their limited precision, the absence of a detec-tion in the brute-force folding search may be due to the original ephemerides no longer accurately describing the systems, for in-stance, as a result of orbital evolution. We therefore conducted an independent search for periodic signals using the PRESTO accelsearch routine, performing Fourier-domain searches for periodicities across all 4000 dedispersed time series generated for each FAST observation. Because the duration of all FAST observations, Tobs , cor-responds to about 30% of the system’s orbital period ( Tobs ≈

0.3PB), significant orbital jerk is expected. Although the PRESTO accelsearch routine includes a jerk search implementation (Andersen & Ransom 2018), substantial spreading of signal power over multiple Fourier bins persists when Tobs ≳ 0.15 PB

due to uncorrected higher-order acceleration e ff ects (Andersen & Ransom 2018). Since there is no quantitative prescription for the loss of Fourier-domain signal power when Tobs /PB ≳ 0.15, we performed searches using both the full observation and seg-mented time series (as in, e.g. Sathyaprakash et al. 2019). This combined strategy balances signal coherence and computational effi ciency, maintaining sensitivity to pulsars in compact binary systems. 1. First, we carried out a Fourier-domain jerk search with 

accelsearch over the full-length time series to maximize sensitivity to weak signals; to limit computational cost, this search was restricted to a narrow frequency range (a few tens of Hz) centred on the known spin frequency of each pulsar. The maximum acceleration and jerk along the line of sight that were searched are 20 m s −2 and 1.2 ×10 −2m s −3

for IGR J00291 +5934, and 24 m s −2 and 4.3 ×10 −2m s −3 for MAXI J1957 +032. 2. Second, we divided each time series into two equal seg-ments such that Tobs /PB ∼ 0.15, and searched each segment with accelsearch . Segmenting the data ensures the valid-ity of the linear-acceleration approximation and preserves signal coherence. Since the radiometer sensitivity scales as 1/ √Tobs , segmenting the observation into two equal-duration data sets results in an approximate 60% reduction in sen-sitivity. However, as noted previously, it remains uncertain how this reduction compares to the loss of signal power expected under the constant-acceleration assumption in the regime Tobs /PB ≳ 0.15 (see e.g., Andersen & Ransom 2018). We searched the segmented time series to higher ac-celeration and jerk values: 26 m s −2 and 2.9 ×10 −2m s −3 for IGR J00291 +5934, and 117 m s −2 and 20.5 ×10 −2m s −3 for MAXI J1957 +032. To limit computational costs, we set the maximum number of harmonics being summed incoherently with accelsearch to two. Fourier-domain detections, characterized by an equivalent Gaussian significance σF 4, greater than 1.5 across all dispersion-measure (DM) trials were sifted to produce final candidate lists.      

> 4Here σFdenotes the equivalent Gaussian significance derived from the false-alarm probability of the measured Fourier power, computed assuming the appropriate χ2distribution and accounting for the number of harmonics summed; see Ransom et al. (2002) for details.

During the sifting stage, detections consistent in spin frequency across multiple DMs were grouped together, and for each group the DM yielding the highest significance was retained while har-monically related and low-significance duplicates were rejected. Candidates with the highest significances ( σF > 5) were subse-quently folded using prepfold . Visual inspection of the result-ing folded profiles did not reveal any convincing pulsar candi-dates. 

> 3.1.3. Brute-force search in the time of ascending node

We searched the dedispersed time series using the 

SPIDER_TWISTER package 5 to correct for possible drift in the pulsar’s time of passage through the ascending node, Tasc .The software performs a grid search over Tasc , folding the data using the reference timing ephemeris for a range of trial values and identifying the one that maximizes the S /N of the folded profile. This method e ffi ciently recovers pulsations in “spider” systems whose orbital periods evolve stochastically and can render older ephemerides inaccurate (Ridolfi et al. 2016). For each source, we used a number of Tasc trials twice that of the minimum required by Eq. 5 of Ridolfi et al. (2016), ensuring complete coverage of plausible orbital-phase o ff sets. We visually inspected the ∼4000 diagnostic prepfold 

plots produced at each trial DM for every observation of both sources. While no promising signal was identified for MAXI J1957 +032, a small number of tentative candidates were found for IGR J00291 +5934 in both observations and war-ranted further scrutiny. We subsequently refolded the raw data at the candidate DMs following the procedure described in Sec-tion 3.1.1, and performed a finer search in the vicinity of each candidate DM. In addition, we explored several combinations of prepfold folding parameters (e.g., number of profile bins, subintegrations, and frequency subbands) to better assess the robustness of the putative detections, as false positives arising from random noise fluctuations are known to be highly sensi-tive to binning choices. The resulting diagnostic plots were re-examined, but no convincing signals were recovered, leading us to conclude that the initially selected candidates are consis-tent with false positives. This interpretation is further supported by the following considerations: (i) none of the candidates ex-hibits a coherent detection across neighbouring or consecutive DM trials, whereas a genuine pulsar signal would typically per-sist across several ( ≳2–3) adjacent DM values; (ii) the apparent signals vanish when alternative prepfold parameter settings are adopted; (iii) the candidates are not statistically significant rela-tive to the distribution of folding statistics obtained from the full set of SPIDER_TWISTER trials; and (iv) no candidate is detected at a consistent DM in both observations.

4. Discussion 

We have carried out the deepest search to date for ra-dio pulsations from the AMSPs IGR J00291 +5934 and MAXI J1957 +032 during their X-ray quiescent phases using 

FAST . For both sources, no definitive pulsed signal was de-tected. For MAXI J1957 +032, this result confirms what was re-cently obtained with four di ff erent FAST observations performed in 2024 by Li et al. (2026). In IGR J00291 +5934, hundreds of marginal candidates have been identified (see Section 3.1.3), but none of them can be reliably excluded as a false positive. These results align with the history of unsuccessful searches for radio pulsations in quiescent AMSPs, which we summarise in Table 5 and Fig. 2. For all those sources, we have estimated the pseudo-luminosity Lpseudo by multiplying the measured ra-dio flux density S ν (or the upper limit on it) by the distance d2,i.e. Lpseudo = S ν × d2. It is noteworthy that the lack of radio pulse detections in quiescent AMSPs is in tension with several (indi-rect) observational clues suggesting that a radio pulsar should be active during quiescence. 4.1. Possible scenarios for the non-detection of radio pulsations. 

A simple scenario that could account for the absence of radio pulsations in our sources is the presence of residual accretion and /or of a disc at the time of the radio observations, since even in tMSPs, radio pulsations are not observed during "disc states", with the most stringent upper limit to date being ∼2 μJy with 

FAST (Baglio et al. 2023). In order to check whether our sources were in quiescence, we performed X-rays and optical observ-ing campaigns within a few days of our FAST observations. The stringent upper limits obtained on their X-ray luminosity of 10 33 

erg s −1 with Swift /XRT are lower than the typical X-ray lumi-nosity of tMSPs in "disc states" (10 33 -10 34 erg s −1 , see e.g. Pa-pitto & de Martino 2022), excluding that this type of activity was ongoing at the time of our FAST visits. Additionally, our optical upper limits of i′ >19.9 - 21.6 mag (AB system) for IGR J00291 +5934 are consistent with the magnitudes reported in previous studies in quiescence (D’Avanzo et al. 2007; Jonker et al. 2008; Baglio et al. 2017), and are ∼ 3 magnitudes fainter than the expected optical magnitude of the source in outburst (i′ ∼ 17 .2 mag near the peak; Lewis et al. 2010). However, we note that IGR J00291 +5934 has displayed flaring activity in the past at magnitudes of about i′ ∼ 20 .7 (Baglio et al. 2017), mostly consistent with or below the upper limits obtained in this work. Since such flaring was attributed to the presence of an accretion disc, we cannot entirely rule out the possibility of a faint, resid-ual accretion disc at the time of our observations. Assuming that these sources were indeed in quiescence at the time of our observing campaigns, a simple scenario ac-counting for the radio non-detection of IGR J00291 +5934 and MAXI J1957 +032 would be that they are exceptionally radio faint. For instance, the "ultra-faint" MSP PSR J0318.1 +0252 dis-covered by FAST in 2018 - one of the faintest MSPs ever detected in radio - would have an observed flux density below those upper limits, if it were located at a distance comparable to the sources in our study (Wang et al. 2021). Furthermore, we note that the pseudo-luminosities measured for the three known tMSPs (see Fig. 2) are largely consistent with the upper limits established for most AMSPs (excluding the sources analysed in this work), suggesting that these objects are comparatively faint in the radio band. Alternatively, we consider the possibility that external fac-tors may have hampered the detection of radio pulsations from our targets. For instance, the pulsar beam may have been un-favourably viewed, with a ∼66% probability that it did not in-tersect our line of sight (Iacolina et al. 2009). Eclipses by the companion star could also contribute to the non-detection; how-ever, this e ff ect is expected to be minimal in our case, as the observations were timed to coincide with the pulsar’s inferior conjunction. Even in the presence of significant orbital evolu-tion between outbursts, as sometimes observed in AMSPs (e.g. Sanna et al. 2017a; Illiano et al. 2023), any resulting shift in the time of passage would be negligible, i.e. within a few percent of the orbital period.

This ef-fect is known to alter drastically the pulsation detectability, even from orbit to orbit (see, e.g. Papitto et al. 2013; Zic et al. 2024, for examples in tMSPs and “spider” pulsars). In AMSPs, such a scenario is particularly plausible if a so-called "radio-ejection" mechanism (consisting in the expulsion of transferred material by the pulsar wind radiation pressure, e.g. Burderi et al. 2001) is active, as supported by recent general relativistic magneto-hydrodynamic (GRMHD) simulations (Parfrey & Tchekhovskoy 2017, 2024) and indirectly evidenced by their occasionally fast orbital expansion (Di Salvo et al. 2008) and low average X-ray luminosity (Marino et al. 2017, 2019). Interestingly, since less material is required to enshroud more compact systems, this e ff ect is expected to anti-correlate with the orbital period (Burgay et al. 2003). A tentative trend is indeed suggested by archival radio searches in AMSPs (Fig. 2 and Ta-ble 5): tMSPs typically have orbital periods longer than ∼4 hr, whereas AMSPs with reported (but unsuccessful) radio pulsation searches generally lie at shorter periods. > 4.2. Do AMSPs simply not transition to the radio pulsar state in quiescence?

Even considering that many factors may have negatively influ-enced our ability to detect pulsations from our target sources, the fact that 9 out of 10 AMSPs observed in the radio band during quiescence (see Table 5) do not show pulsations begs to con-sider the alternative scenario where these sources may simply not switch to active radio pulsars when accretion stops. As a con-sequence, the secular spin-down observed in several AMSPs that displayed more than one outbursts could not be explained with radio pulsar-like behaviour in quiescence, suggesting that other mechanisms, such as, e.g., residual torques on a cold disc or a weak propeller phase, have to be responsible (see Di Salvo & Sanna 2022, and references therein). If AMSPs and tMSPs are indeed distinct classes, the phys-ical origin of this dichotomy remains unclear, whether it arises from di ff erences in the long-term mass-accretion rate, the nature of the companion star, and /or the microphysics of the coherent radio emission mechanism itself, which remains poorly under-stood. One distinction between the two classes seems to be the long-term behavior of their X-ray emission; the prolonged, sub-luminous X-ray active states observed in several candidate or confirmed tMSPs (e.g. Bogdanov et al. 2015; Coti Zelati et al. 2021; Baglio et al. 2023) are indeed strikingly distinct from the shorter but significantly brighter outbursts typically observed in AMSPs, suggesting di ff erences in the physical conditions in the accretion flows among the two classes. However, we note that this distinction is not unambiguous considering that: i) the mere existence of IGR J18245 −2452 as a tMSP that underwent a sin-gle, bright AMSP outburst (Papitto et al. 2013) proves that some sources can exhibit both types of X-ray activity; ii) "disc states" 

à la tMSPs are intrinsically faint, with X-ray luminosities of the order 10 34 erg /s (Papitto & de Martino 2022) and might thus be challenging to catch with all-sky X-ray monitors; iii) faint X-

5. Conclusions 

In this manuscript, we have conducted one of the deepest searches for radio pulsations in L-band ever performed for quies-cent AMSPs, using the unprecedented capabilities of FAST . The target sources, i.e., IGR J00291 +5934 and MAXI J1957 +032, along with the intermittent AMSP Aql X −1, are the only AMSPs known so far that are visible at the telescope site. Nevertheless, neither targets were detected as a radio pulsator. Using the ra-diometer equation and assuming a pulse duty cycle of 10%, we have set a stringent upper limit of ∼3-6 μJy on the pulsed radio emission from both these sources, the deepest up to date for any AMSP. These limits are consistent with what was reported by Peng et al. (2025) for the intermittent AMSP Aql X −1, also ob-served with FAST . While FAST is not suited to observe the major-ity of AMSPs, which are predominantly located in the southern hemisphere, high-cadence monitoring with existing, even less powerful, telescopes remains a feasible avenue considering that pulse detectability may vary from orbit to orbit. Observations with future facilities of comparable or improved sensitivity, such as the Square Kilometre Array (SKA), will further help provide more definitive answers on whether some or all known AMSPs can turn on as radio pulsators during quiescence.
