## Trace anomaly and interior curvature of neutron stars in energy-momentum squared gravity 

Ratikanta Swain ID ,∗ Sayantan Ghosh ID ,† and Bharat Kumar ID ‡

Department of Physics and Astronomy, National Institute of Technology, Rourkela 769008, India 

(Dated: June 19, 2026) In energy-momentum squared gravity (EMSG), the spacetime inside a neutron star is sourced by effective thermodynamic variables that need not coincide with the physical fluid pressure and energy density. It is there-fore an open question whether the trace anomaly of dense matter—the QCD measure of how strongly conformal symmetry is broken—still organizes interior profiles and curvature in the same way it does in general relativ-ity (GR). We adopt a clear matter–geometry separation: the trace anomaly is computed from the fluid sector alone, while spacetime curvature scalars are built from the variables that actually source the modified Tolman-Oppenheimer-Volkoff equations. For five relativistic mean-field equations of state, the radial trace-anomaly profiles increase monotonically from core to surface in all accepted EMSG models, as in GR, but split systemat-ically with the EMSG coupling strength; the splitting grows with stellar compactness. Despite this deformation, curvature invariants still fall onto organized bands when plotted against the trace anomaly, extending the GR thermodynamic-geometric correspondence. The Ricci contraction shows the tightest organization, whereas the Ricci scalar remains the most equation-of-state sensitive. EMSG effects are modest for observationally acces-sible stars but largest in stiff, ultracompact configurations, indicating that the trace anomaly remains a useful thermodynamic label for interior geometry even when gravity couples nonlinearly to matter. 

I. INTRODUCTION 

Neutron stars (NSs) are unique laboratories for matter under extreme conditions[1–4]. Formed in core-collapse supernovae[5–7], they compress matter to several times nu-clear saturation density in cores only a few tens of kilome-ters across[8–12]. Their hydrostatic equilibrium is governed by the Tolman–Oppenheimer–Volkoff (TOV) equations[13, 14], supplemented by an equation of state (EOS) that en-codes QCD matter, nuclear interactions, and possible exotic phases[4, 15–18]. In this sense, NSs probe both the cold EOS of dense matter and general relativity (GR) far beyond solar-system tests[19–22]. The observational landscape has transformed dramatically over the past decade. Radio timing of millisecond pul-sars, including massive objects such as PSR J0740 +6620 and PSR J0952 −0607, has established that NSs can reach masses above 2 M⊙[11, 12, 23–25], placing strong lower bounds on the maximum mass supported by the EOS. X-ray pulse-profile modeling with NICER has begun to constrain mass– radius relations for individual pulsars[11, 12, 23, 26], while the gravitational-wave event GW170817[27–30] provided the first direct access to tidal deformability and hence to the com-pactness of canonical-mass stars[31–34]. Follow-up analyses combining nuclear experiments, chiral effective field theory, and astrophysical data have narrowed the space of viable EOS models[34–38]. At the same time, precision tests of GR with binary pulsars[9, 39] and the emerging possibility of measur-ing NS moment of inertia through pulsar timing[40–43] have renewed interest in whether NS phenomenology can discrim-inate among gravitational theories[44–50]. Interpreting such rich data is complicated by the fact that most interior quantities depend sensitively on the EOS. Stan-

A. Trace anomaly and curvature in General Relativity 

The trace anomaly ∆ [Eq. (25)] measures how far cold dense matter departs from the conformal symmetry expected of massless QCD[53, 56, 58]. Running couplings and finite quark masses break this symmetry at the densities reached in NS cores, so perturbative QCD predicts that the anomaly vanishes only at asymptotically high density[72–77]. Fuji-moto et al. [58] proposed the normalized trace anomaly as a dimensionless measure of conformality and showed that the speed of sound can be decomposed into contributions tied to 

∆, making the latter more informative about the approach to the conformal limit than the instantaneous value of c2 

> s

alone. Thermodynamic stability and causality restrict ∆ to the range 

−2/3 ≤ ∆ < 1/3 in NS matter[53, 58], and three recent GR developments motivate the present study. First, Cai et al. [56, 57] showed from the intrinsic struc-ture of the TOV equations that the pressure-to-energy ratio decreases monotonically outward from the stellar center for any viable static solution, independently of the input EOS.

> B. The EMSG complication

Energy-Momentum Squared Gravity (EMSG) modifies GR by coupling the spacetime curvature to the square of the mat-ter stress–energy[64, 65, 68, 69, 78, 79]. The theory coincides with GR in vacuum but introduces density-dependent correc-tions inside matter, where the coupling constant α is con-strained to be extremely small by cosmological and binary-pulsar data[68, 80, 81] yet can alter NS structure when cen-tral densities are high[69, 82–84]. For a perfect fluid the field equations can be recast in GR-like form with effective pres-sure and energy density [Eqs. (16)–(17)] that differ from the physical fluid variables. Existing EMSG studies have focused on modified mass– radius relations, tidal deformability, f -mode frequencies, and binary-pulsar bounds[65, 66, 69, 85–89]. Most recently, Ghosh et al. [71] computed the Kretschmann, Ricci, and Weyl scalars in EMSG as functions of baryon density and radius, showing that positive (negative) α enhances (suppresses) cur-vature and that hadron–quark phase transitions can leave dis-tinct imprints in the curvature profiles. That work established how effective sourcing reshapes the interior curvature struc-ture, but it did not ask whether the fluid-sector trace anomaly 

∆, defined exactly as in GR, still correlates with curvature when the two sectors are treated separately. Nor did it test the trace-anomaly–observable organization of Ren and Lin[53] or the curvature– ∆ correspondence of Garibay et al. [54] beyond 

α = 0 . The gap is conceptual as well as numerical: if one replaces the fluid variables by the effective ones in the defini-tion of ∆, the thermodynamic label itself changes and direct comparison with the GR literature is lost. The central ques-tion addressed here is therefore the following. When grav-ity is sourced by effective variables in EMSG, does the trace anomaly ∆, defined only from the fluid sector, still provide an organizing parameter for interior spacetime curvature?  

> C. Strategy and main results of this work

Our approach is deliberately asymmetric and follows the matter–geometry separation introduced in Sec. II E: ∆ is com-puted from the fluid sector alone, whereas the curvature invari-ants K, W, R, and F are built from the effective variables that source the modified TOV equations. Any correlation between curvature and ∆ in EMSG is therefore nontrivial, because the horizontal axis carries a theory-independent thermodynamic label while the vertical axis reflects modified effective sourc-ing [Eqs. (26)–(27)]. Solving the modified TOV system for the five relativis-tic mean-field EOSs of Sec. II G (NL3, IOPB-I, G3, SINPA, GM1), we find: 1. In all accepted EMSG integrations, the radial profile of ∆ increases monotonically outward and remains EOS structured, numerically mirroring the outward-increasing GR trend of Cai et al. [56] without invoking their GR-only proof, but acquires an α-dependent split-ting that grows with compactness and is strongest for smaller tidal deformability and smaller normalized mo-ment of inertia. 2. Curvature invariants collapse onto organized one-parameter bands when plotted against ∆, extending the GR curvature– ∆ correspondence of Garibay et al. [54] and complementing the I–Love–curvature relations of Danarianto et al. [55] in a modified-gravity setting. 3. EMSG corrections are largest for ultracompact config-urations, yet remain smooth and regular over the cou-pling interval of Eq. (39). 4. At fixed compactness, tidal deformability, and moment of inertia, the α-induced splitting of ∆( r) remains mod-est for observationally accessible parameters, while the Ricci contraction shows the tightest organization among the four invariants. These findings show that nonlinear gravity deforms, but does not erase, the thermodynamic–geometric organization already established in GR. The remainder of the paper is organized as follows. > C. Spacetime curvature invariants

For the static, spherically symmetric line element of Eq. (18), the Riemann tensor yields coordinate-independent scalars that diagnose the local geometry[54, 71, 99–105]. We employ the Kretschmann scalar K ≡ pRμνρσ Rμνρσ , the Weyl scalar W ≡ pCμνρσ Cμνρσ , the Ricci scalar R ≡

gμν Rμν , and the Ricci contraction F ≡ pRμν Rμν , follow-ing the conventions of Garibay et al. [54] and Ghosh et al. [71]. In GR these quantities are fully determined by (E, P ) through the field equations[14, 98]. In EMSG they are evaluated from 

(Eeff , P eff ) because the metric responds to the effective source. All curvature quantities in what follows are computed from 

Eeff and Peff , reflecting the nonlinear response of spacetime to matter[69, 71, 84]. The correction terms scale as αE2 and therefore grow most rapidly in the dense core, where even cos-mologically small α can produce order-unity shifts in the in-variants at fixed ρB [69, 82, 84].

> E. Matter–geometry separation

This subsection states the conceptual framework that distin-guishes the present analysis from prior work. In GR, Garibay 

et al. [54] correlated curvature invariants with ∆ because both sectors are sourced by the same (E, P ). Ren and Lin[53] linked the profile ∆( r) to global observables without invoking 5modified gravity. Ghosh et al. [71] computed EMSG curvature profiles as functions of baryon density and radius, but did not hold the GR definition of ∆ fixed while testing whether geom-etry still tracks it. Here we combine these threads under one protocol. In EMSG the metric is sourced by T eff  

> μν

[Eq. (14)], not by the fluid tensor Tμν of Eq. (12). The effective variables 

(Eeff , P eff ) in Eqs. (16)–(17) encode how nonlinear gravity re-sponds to matter, whereas (E, P ) continue to describe the ther-modynamic state of the stellar fluid itself. Treating these two sets interchangeably would mix modified-gravity effects into the definition of ∆ and obscure the central question posed in Sec. I. We therefore adopt an explicit two-sector protocol: • Matter sector (theory independent). The trace anomaly is evaluated only from the fluid variables 

(E, P ) through Eq. (25). Because ∆ depends solely on the ratio P/ E, it carries the same interpretation in GR and EMSG: it measures how far the local fluid departs from conformal symmetry. No α-dependent correction enters its definition. • Geometry sector (theory dependent). Curvature in-variants are constructed from the metric generated by 

(Eeff , P eff ) through Eqs. (21)–(24). Symbolically, 

I(r) = G(Eeff (r), P eff (r)) , I ∈ {K , W, R, F} . (26) In EMSG this sector is genuinely modified: Eeff ̸ = E

and Peff ̸ = P whenever α̸ = 0 .The separation is not merely notational. A quantity such as 

R = κ(Eeff − 3Peff ) [Eq. (23)] resembles a trace-anomaly structure, but it is built from effective variables and therefore measures curvature sourced by T eff  

> μν

, not the trace anomaly of Eq. (25). Likewise, replacing (E, P ) by (Eeff , P eff ) in the definition of ∆ would redefine the thermodynamic label itself and prevent a direct comparison with GR studies[53, 54, 56]. Collecting both sectors, each curvature invariant is a com-posite function of the trace anomaly, the coupling α, and the EOS: 

I = f (∆; α, EOS) , (27) because Eqs. (16)–(17) make (Eeff , P eff ) nonlinear functions of (E, P ) at fixed α. In GR, Eeff = E and Peff = P , so organized I(∆) bands are expected from prior work[54]. In EMSG the correspondence is not guaranteed a priori : effec-tive sourcing can in principle decouple geometry from the thermodynamic state even when ∆ remains well defined. The numerical program of Sec. III is therefore a direct test of whether Eq. (27) remains sufficiently tight, across five RMF EOSs and the coupling range quoted in Sec. II H, for ∆ to serve as a reduced coordinate for interior spacetime geometry.

> G. Equation of state models

We employ five relativistic mean-field (RMF) EOSs span-ning soft to stiff behavior: NL3[113, 114], IOPB-I[115, 116], G3[117, 118], SINPA[119], and GM1[120]. These models are widely used in NS structure studies[18, 110, 111, 121, 122] and match the hadronic set employed in recent EMSG curva-ture analyses[71]. They cover the stiffness range over which Garibay et al. [54] reported the strongest R–∆ correlations in GR.

> III. RESULTS AND DISCUSSION

We organize the numerical results around the central ques-tion of Sec. I. Section III A establishes how EMSG reshapes the curvature invariants as functions of baryon density, ex-tending the density-based analysis of Ghosh et al. [71] but preparing the I(∆) test of Sec. III E. Section III B exam-ines the radial profiles and compares them with the GR I– Love–curvature trends of Danarianto et al. [55]. Sections III C and III D study the fluid-sector trace anomaly ∆( r) and its re-sponse to α at fixed (C, Λ, ¯I), directly confronting the quasi-universal relations of Ren and Lin[53] and the monotonicity theorems of Cai et al. [56]. Section III E performs the prin-cipal test: whether Garibay et al. ’s[54] GR correspondence between curvature and ∆ survives when geometry is sourced by (Eeff , P eff ). Finally, Sec. III F places the EMSG bands in the observational context of NICER and GW170817.  

> A. Curvature scalars as functions of baryon density

Figure 1 shows the curvature invariants as functions of baryon density ρB for the five RMF EOSs at representa-tive values of α. In GR, Garibay et al. > B. Radial profiles of curvature invariants

Figures 2 and 3 display the radial profiles of the four in-variants for each EOS, with colored curves spanning the cou-8pling range of Eq. (39) and dashed curves showing the GR limit α = 0 . Danarianto et al. [55] reported in GR that K

peaks at the center while W peaks in the outer layers, and that volume-averaged curvature scalars correlate strongly with ¯I

and Λ. The EMSG profiles preserve this qualitative order-ing but acquire an α-dependent shift because the metric re-sponds to T eff  

> μν

. The Kretschmann scalar is maximal at the center and decreases monotonically outward, with Kc ranging from ∼ 5 × 10 −8 m−2 to ∼ 7.5 × 10 −8 m−2 depending on the EOS and on α. This falloff follows from Eq. (21): the matter terms ∝ (E2eff + P 2eff ) peak where the effective energy density peaks, while the exterior approaches the vacuum limit 

K2surf = 48 M 2/r 6[55]. Positive α uniformly enhances cen-tral K, whereas negative α suppresses it; the separation be-tween colored EMSG curves and the dashed α = 0 baselines is largest for NL3 and GM1, as expected from the O(αE2)

structure of the effective source[69, 71, 82]. The Weyl scalar shows the opposite radial trend. It vanishes at the center because m(r)/r 3 → 4πEeff /3 as r → 0 for regu-lar spherical solutions[55, 101], then peaks near the surface at 

r/R ≳ 0.95 with Wmax ∼ (1 .2–1.6) × 10 −8 m−2. This outer peak marks the region that dominantly controls the tidal Love number through the I–Love– Q relations[40, 44, 86, 125]. EMSG preserves the GR picture—tidal curvature in the en-velope, Ricci-dominated core—but shifts the amplitude of both invariants through the modified effective variables, so a change in α alters the outer-layer geometry that enters Λ

through Eqs. (29)–(33). The Ricci scalar is strongly EOS dependent and can change sign within the interior for stiff models, signaling Peff /Eeff >

1/3[54, 58]. The radial location of the sign change differs between EOSs because the nuclear symmetry-energy slope shifts the balance between Eeff and Peff [113, 116]. The Ricci contraction F remains positive definite and decreases out-ward, with central values Fc ∼ (3 .8–5.5) × 10 −8 m−2. Be-cause W depends on the difference between m(r)/r 3 and Eeff ,modified gravity can redistribute the radial location of the tidal peak even when the central K change is modest; this helps explain why the Λ- and ¯I-selected sequences in Sec. III D re-spond differently to α. All four invariants vary smoothly with 

α over the interval of Eq. (39), indicating that the EMSG so-lutions remain regular throughout the explored range[69, 71].  

> C. Pressure-to-energy ratio and trace anomaly profiles

We now turn from the geometry sector to the fluid-sector trace anomaly. Figure 4 shows the radial evolution of ϕ(r) = 

P (r)/E(r) and ∆( r) = 1 /3 − ϕ(r) for each EOS at rep-resentative values of α. Cai et al. [56, 57] proved in GR that ϕ must decrease outward from the center for any vi-able static solution, so ∆ attains a minimum in the core; Ren and Lin[53] showed that the full profile is nevertheless orga-nized by global observables to within about 10% EOS scat-ter. The question here is whether the modified TOV system preserves that GR morphology numerically while introducing an α-dependent redistribution of (E, P ). For all EOSs and every accepted (EOS , α ) solution in our scan, ϕ = P/ E de-creases monotonically from the center toward the surface and 

∆ increases outward, reproducing the GR trend established by Cai et al. [56, 57] but without invoking their GR-only proof in modified gravity. Because ∆ is evaluated from the unmod-ified fluid variables, the EMSG coupling does not alter its definition; any α dependence enters only through the mod-ified hydrostatic equilibrium that redistributes (E, P ). Posi-tive α tends to deepen the central gravitational potential and compress the core, increasing ϕc and driving ∆c more nega-tive for stiff EOSs, whereas negative α produces the opposite trend[69, 82, 85]. The central values are strongly EOS dependent. NL3 reaches the stiffest core with ϕc ≳ 0.50 and ∆c ≲ −0.15 ;GM1 follows with ϕc ∼ 0.45 and ∆c ∼ − 0.10 –0.12 ; G3 is intermediate; whereas softer EOSs such as IOPB-I and SINPA remain near ϕc ∼ 0.28 –0.35 with ∆c ∼ +0 .02 –0.05 [54, 58]. Fujimoto et al. [58] conjectured that ∆ may remain nonnega-tive in NS matter, but subsequent ensemble studies by Garibay 

et al. [54] and Ren and Lin[53] showed that stiff EOSs can produce negative ∆c, with the central value passing through zero near zero-curvature compactness C0 = 0.26911 ±

0.00004 [54] and C ≃ 0.26 –0.28 [53, 56]. NL3 and GM1 are also the most sensitive to α because their large central E max-imizes the αE2 source term[18, 69, 85]. Near the surface, 

ϕ → 0 and ∆ → 1/3 for every EOS, confirming that the outer envelope approaches the dilute limit independently of core stiffness and modified-gravity coupling.

Ren and Lin[53] demonstrated that fixing any one of C,

Λ, or ¯I organizes the full radial profile X(r/R ) = P/ E—and hence ∆( r)—to within about 10% across 45 observation-ally viable GR EOSs. Here we repeat that exercise in EMSG: for each target observable we map to a central pressure using GR reference integrations (Sec. II F), then solve the modified TOV system at each (EOS , α ) and extract ∆( r) from the fluid variables alone. The EMSG coupling therefore enters only through the reshaped hydrostatic equilibrium, not through a redefinition of ∆. Figures 5–7 present the resulting profile bands; positive α generally increases ∆( r/R ) relative to the GR baseline (dashed curves), whereas negative α decreases it, with the largest departures appearing in the intermediate region 0.3 ≲ r/R ≲ 0.8.

> F. Observational sequences and ultracompact limits

Figures 10 and 11 combine the compactness, tidal, and moment-of-inertia sequences in a format suited to com-parison with the observational applications of Ren and Lin[53]. The realistic panels use the same (C, Λ, ¯I) targets as Figs. 5–7; the theoretical panels use more compact se-quences with (C, Λ, ¯I) = (0 .256 , 0.267 , 0.277) , (12 , 22 , 32) ,and (7 .5, 8.5, 9.5) , where EMSG profile splitting is strongest. In the realistic panels of Fig. 10, increasing C drives the pro-files toward more negative central ∆, consistent with Fig. 5 and with the GR analyses of Cai et al. [56, 57] and Ren and Lin[53]. Ren and Lin applied their quasi-universal surfaces to NICER targets PSR J0030+0451 and PSR J0740+6620 and inferred ∆c = 0.1770 +0 .0365  

> −0.0432

for a 1.4 M⊙ star from the GW170817 tidal constraint. Our EMSG sequences at comparable Λ remain consistent with this range for soft-to-intermediate EOSs, while stiff models with negative ∆c

NICER radii and GW Λ measurements therefore constrain ∆c indi-rectly in GR; Fig. 10 shows how EMSG widens the allow-able band through α without changing the monotonic radial shape[11, 12, 23, 31, 34]. In the ultracompact regime of Fig. 11, the colored α

Even here, however, the radial ordering of the pro-files is unchanged and no irregular features appear. Existing bounds on α from cosmology and binary pulsars[68, 80, 89] remain far tighter than what the present profile splitting alone would require; the ultracompact panels should therefore be read as illustrating the direction of the EMSG effect rather than as an independent constraint on α. 

> IV. SCOPE AND LIMITATIONS

Several simplifying assumptions bound the scope of the present analysis and should be kept in mind when interpret-ing the results.  

> 1. Equation-of-state set

We employ five hadronic relativistic mean-field EOSs with-out explicit phase transitions, hyperonic degrees of freedom, or quark matter. The R(∆) branch structure and the magni-tude of ultracompact profile splitting are therefore EOS de-pendent, and extending the study to hybrid or quark-hadron models[16, 123, 124] is a natural next step.  

> 2. Perturbative approximations

All models are static, spherically symmetric, and nonrotat-ing. Tidal deformability and normalized moment of inertia enter through the GR perturbation formalism of Sec. II F; the sequence targets (C, Λ, ¯I) are mapped to central conditions with GR reference integrations before the EMSG background profiles are evaluated. A fully self-consistent EMSG treat-ment of tidal and rotational perturbations would modify both the background and the response sector and could alter the quoted α splitting at fixed global observables.  

> 3. Coupling parameter and observational bounds

The coupling interval of Eq. (39) is chosen to bracket prior EMSG neutron-star analyses[69, 71, 82]. Cosmological and binary-pulsar constraints on α are typically far tighter than the profile splitting alone would require[68, 80, 89]. V. SUMMARY AND CONCLUSION 

We have investigated whether the trace anomaly ∆, de-fined only from the fluid sector, continues to organize in-terior spacetime geometry when neutron stars are modeled in energy-momentum squared gravity (EMSG). Adopting the matter–geometry separation of Sec. II E, we computed ∆ from the physical fluid variables and constructed the curvature in-variants from the effective variables that source the modified TOV equations. For five relativistic mean-field equations of state over the EMSG coupling range studied in Sec. II H, three main conclusions emerge. 15 First, the fluid-sector trace-anomaly profiles increase monotonically from core to surface and remain equation-of-state structured in all accepted EMSG integrations, numeri-cally mirroring the outward-increasing GR trend established model-independently by Cai et al. [56, 57] and the quasi-universal profile organization of Ren and Lin[53], but they acquire a coupling-dependent splitting that grows with com-pactness and is strongest for stars with smaller tidal deforma-bility and smaller normalized moment of inertia. Second, the curvature invariants evolve smoothly with baryon den-sity and radius, preserve the GR radial ordering in which the Kretschmann scalar peaks in the core and the Weyl scalar is concentrated in the outer layers[55], and respond con-tinuously to the EMSG coupling throughout the explored range[69, 71]. Third, and most importantly, the invariants nevertheless collapse onto organized one-parameter bands when plotted against ∆, extending the GR thermodynamic– geometric correspondence of Garibay et al. [54] into modified gravity. The Ricci contraction shows the tightest grouping; the Ricci scalar retains the largest equation-of-state sensitivity in the negative-∆ core, consistent with the R–∆ scatter reported by Garibay et al. [54]. Nonlinear gravity therefore shifts spacetime curvature with-out erasing the thermodynamic ordering already established in GR. The effect concentrates in stiff, ultracompact configu-rations, while observationally accessible sequences matched to NICER and GW170817 show only moderate coupling-induced deformation of the trace-anomaly profiles. Multimes-senger constraints on mass, radius, and tidal deformability[11, 12, 31, 34, 35], together with existing EMSG bounds on α

from cosmology and binary pulsars[68, 80, 89], can refine this picture once a fully self-consistent EMSG perturbation treat-ment and a broader equation-of-state ensemble are in place (Sec. IV). Treating the trace anomaly and curvature invari-ants as complementary interior descriptors offers a practi-cal framework for comparing GR and modified-gravity in-terpretations of future pulsar, X-ray, and gravitational-wave data[25, 39, 126–130].
